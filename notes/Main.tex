\paragraph{What is this thesis about and why should anyone care?}


Stories of sleep are as old as time. In Norse mythology, Nótt would ride over the sky on her horse Rimfakse and lull us to sleep by turning day into night. Our ancestors would tell stories of prophetic volver who could change the course of time by going into trance-like states of sleep, and Odin himself would use deep sleep to gather wisdom. Dreams were seen as premonitions, and sleep held an important role in the public mind. Fast forward a thousand years and we still recognise sleep is a fundamental biological process that supports cognitive and emotional functioning. This thesis is built on sleep as a multifaceted construct that varies from night to night. Even in healthy adults, sleep duration and quality will differ across individuals and across time, shaped by circadian rhythms, lifestyle factors, and environmental demands \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Habitual sleep patterns may reflect relatively stable individual differences, whereas nightly deviations can reflect transient changes in arousal, fatigue, and regulatory capacity \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Understanding how these different aspects of sleep relate to cognition is important because everyday performance often depends on long-term sleep habits as well as how well one slept in the days leading up to a demanding situation.


As mentioned earlier, there is an extensive body of research linking sleep and executive function, but there appear to be two conceptual gaps that are relevant to this thesis. First, many studies treat sleep as a single predictor (e.g., ``sleep duration'') rather than a multidimensional phenomenon consisting of additional factors like efficiency, continuity, and variability. Only looking at one aspect of sleep can obscure meaningful differences between individuals who sleep the same number of hours but differ in sleep fragmentation or efficiency. Second, even when objective sleep measures are available, fewer studies explicitly distinguish between habitual sleep level (how a person typically sleeps over days or weeks) and sleep variability (how much a person's sleep fluctuates from night to night). These two aspects may have different cognitive correlates. Habitual sleep may capture more stable sleep-related characteristics, whereas variability may reflect dysregulation or instability in sleep timing and quality that could be relevant for cognitive control \parencite{fjell2024sleep}.

\paragraph{What is cognitive control and why is flexibility a meaningful construct?}

A post-doc in the search of a good reading spot enters a café. She orders a cappuccino, and sits down to start grading a fresh master thesis about sleep and cognitive control. The interior landscape is filled the hustle and bustle of lively conversations, chairs scraping against the floor, and children running around. She feels a short pang of regret about her choice of scene, but decides to power through the paper anyway. After a short while she zooms into her reading material and suppresses the surrounding distractions in order to focus on the text. When the waitress arrives with her coffee, she briefly shifts her attention to thank her before returning to reading. In this situation, adapted from \textcite{egner2023principles}, the post-doc relies on a set of mental operations that allow her to maintain task goals, prioritize relevant information, inhibit competing inputs, and adjust behaviour as circumstances change. These processes are known as \emph{cognitive control} or \emph{executive functions}.

Although we do use these functions in everyday life, they are especially important in navigating more demanding and novel situations, such as finding the right gate in an unfamiliar airport, planning tasks or solving problems creatively \parencite{diamond2013executive, frith2021creativity}. When our executive functions fail, we can experience glitches like neglecting to thank the waitress for her service. Lapses in attention, difficulties updating behaviour, or an inability to suppress irrelevant information can also have more serious consequences like impairing decision-making in contexts ranging from academic performance to clinical judgement and traffic safety. Understanding the mechanisms that support flexible, goal-directed behaviour is a central challenge in cognitive neuroscience.

\subsection{Frameworks of cognitive control}

Executive functions are often conceptualized as higher-order processes that enable individuals to regulate thoughts and actions in accordance with internal goals. A highly influential account is the unity and diversity framework proposed by \textcite{friedman2017unity}, which reconciles two seemingly competing views: that executive functions reflect a common control capacity, and that they consist of partially separable components. According to this model, tasks requiring inhibition, shifting, and updating share variance because they draw on a domain-general ability to actively maintain goals and bias processing toward task-relevant information. At the same time, each component captures additional processes that are not fully reducible to this shared control factor. Shifting tasks, for example, involve mechanisms related specifically to disengaging from one task set and activating another. This perspective implies that cognitive control can not be completely explained as a single unified faculty, nor is it a collection of entirely independent skills. Instead it reflects coordinated processes that operate together to support adaptive behaviour. This coordination comes into play in situations that require balancing persistence with adaptability, such as maintaining a current goal (reading an article) while remaining prepared to modify it when the environment changes (saying thank you for coffee).

Building on this idea, contemporary accounts describe cognition as a dynamic interplay between stability and flexibility. Rather than representing opposite ends of a continuum, these properties can be understood as complementary demands that are regulated in accordance with situational requirements \parencite{egner2023principles}. Returning to the café example, the post-doc needs to sustain attention on the article despite ongoing distraction, reflecting stability. At the same time she also has to remain capable of shifting attention when the waitress approaches, reflecting flexibility. Adaptive behaviour emerges from efficiently coordinating when to maintain a goal state and when to update it.
Mechanistically, this coordination has been described in terms of biasing and gating processes within working memory. Biasing prioritizes currently relevant representations, protecting them from interference, whereas gating determines when new information is allowed to enter and update the active task set. In this view, cognitive flexibility depends on the ability to regulate control processes in a context-sensitive manner, instead of responding quickly to every stimulus. A study by \textcite{cools2019gatingmechanisms} lends neurobiological support to Egner's model of flexibility and stability as separate but coordinated processes. Drawing on pharmacological neuroimaging studies, Cools proposes that prefrontal dopamine supports the stable maintenance of task representations, whereas striatal dopamine enables flexible updating through different gating mechanisms. Adaptive cognition then becomes dependent on regulating the balance between persistence and updating, rather than shifting along a single control dimension.

The dual mechanisms of control (DMC, \cite{braver2012dualmechanisms}) framework offers a mechanistic account of executive function by distinguishing between two temporal modes of control, proactive and reactive. Proactive control refers to the sustained, anticipatory maintenance of task goals to bias perception and action before an event occurs, whereas reactive control reflects a just-in-time reactivation of goals when interference or conflict is detected. These modes differ in timing and in cognitive demands. Proactive control is more effortful and resource-dependent, and reactive control is relatively low-cost and stimulus-driven. If you look at the unity–diversity model in relation to DMC, it highlights that the same executive task can rely on multiple underlying control modes, and that individuals may differ in the extent to which they engage proactive versus reactive strategies. This distinction could help us understand variability in executive functioning across time. Because proactive control depends on sustained goal maintenance, it is more vulnerable to fluctuations in arousal, attentional capacity, and fatigue, whereas reactive control tends to remain stable under conditions such as sleep deprivation. In this context, sleep becomes a theoretically meaningful source of variation, like we saw in the study of \cite{kusztor2019differentialsleep}. Poor or irregular sleep impaired proactive, top-down control processes more strongly than automatic, reactive ones. This could potentially reveal something about which components of a task reflect stable trait-like abilities and which reflect state-dependent fluctuations. In this way, the DMC framework can be used to clarify some of the mechanisms underlying executive performance and provide a basis for predicting how habitual and nightly sleep variability should differentially influence the processes engaged during tasks such as task switching.

Considered alongside the unity–diversity framework, the DMC account suggests that executive tasks rarely rely on a single control process. Instead, performance reflects the extent to which individuals engage proactive versus reactive strategies, as well as their ability to flexibly shift between them. Two individuals may therefore achieve similar behavioural outcomes through partially different control dynamics. Sleep variability may contribute to systematic fluctuations in how cognitive control is implemented rather than degrading performance overall.

\paragraph{How do researchers operationalize flexibility?}

Task-switching paradigms are often used to investigate cognitive flexibility because they capture the ability to disengage from one task set and configure another in response to changing demands \parencite{monsell2003switching,kiesel2010review}. In the task switching paradigms like the one used in this thesis, participants alternate between simple classification rules. Typically they will produce slower and less accurate responses on rule switching trials than on repetition trials. This switch cost is generally interpreted as reflecting the cognitive effort required to reconfigure task representations and overcome interference from the previously relevant rule. Some task-switching designs also manipulate stimulus congruency. Congruent stimuli afford the same response across task rules, whereas incongruent stimuli activate competing response mappings. That is, if a task is congruent it will elicit the same response, like a left button press, for both task rules. If the stimuli is incongruent it will elicit different responses for the different task rules, like a left button click for one rule and a right button click for the other. Congruency effects provide an additional measure of interference control to the switch cost in the set shifting task. Interference is often amplified on switch trials, suggesting that the need to update task sets may temporarily increase susceptibility to competing stimulus–response associations \parencite{kiesel2010review}.

\paragraph{How do we measure control in the brain?}
At the neural level, cognitive flexibility is supported by a distributed frontoparietal control network involving medial and lateral prefrontal regions as well as posterior parietal cortex \parencite{kiesel2010review}. These regions are associated with goal maintenance, attentional allocation, and the resolution of interference, which is needed when behaviour is reconfigured quickly in response to changing task demands. Successful set shifting depends on the ability to represent multiple task rules, as well as mechanisms that monitor conflict and signal the need for increased control.

Electrophysiological (EEG) research is used to investigate these mechanisms. Frontal midline theta (FM-theta) is a rhythmic activity typically observed in the 4–8 Hz range over medial frontal scalp sites. It has been suggested as a neural marker of cognitive control. Findings from EEG and intracranial recordings suggest that FM-theta is primarily generated in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and adjacent medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), regions implicated in performance monitoring and the dynamic allocation of control \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,depestele2023thetadriving}. FM-theta is thought to index a domain-general control signal that becomes stronger when ongoing behaviour requires adjustment. Consistent with this interpretation, FM-theta power increases during conditions that place elevated demands on control, including response conflict, error commission, and task switching. Switch trials and incongruent trials often elicit stronger theta activity than low-conflict conditions, suggesting that medial frontal systems register the need for enhanced control when competing task sets or response tendencies are activated. In this sense, FM-theta can be understood as a neural signature of control engagement during flexible behaviour.

Research indicates that this control-related neural activity is sensitive to sleep. Experimental sleep deprivation has been associated with diminished theta responses during cognitively demanding tasks, alongside behavioural impairments in executive functioning \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep,wilckens2014sleeppreparation,wilckens2020sleepmddswitch}. Findings like this support the view that insufficient or disrupted sleep may compromise the neural systems responsible for monitoring performance and recruiting control. Altogether, these observations motivate the use of FM-theta as a neural outcome in the present thesis. If sleep contributes to the stability of cognitive control, individual differences in habitual sleep and sleep variability may be reflected both in behavioural performance and in the magnitude of control-related theta activity. FM-theta may therefore function as a state-sensitive neural marker linking multidimensional sleep patterns to the efficiency with which control is engaged during task switching.

\paragraph{Why might cognitive control vary instead of being fixed?}
 Sleep disturbances are associated with impairments in executive functions \parencite{sen2023sleepef,tai2022sleepef, slama2018sleepswitching, wilckens2020sleepmddswitch, wilckens2014sleeppreparation, fjell2024sleep, kusztor2019differentialsleep, wilckens2016sleepcognitionage, basner2013sleepcognition, couyoumdjian2010switchingsleep}. The relationship between sleep and executive functioning appears to be selective in the sense that experimental sleep deprivation appears to impair some cognitive operations while sparing others \parencite{killgore2010sleepcognition}. Studies show that top-down control processes are more strongly distrupted than relatively automatic or stimulus-driven processes \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep}. This pattern suggests that sleep may be more relevant for cognitive control demands that require maintaining and updating task goals, resolving interference, and flexibly adjusting behavior to changing task requirements. A central component of cognitive control is cognitive flexibility, the ability to adapt behaviour when goals or task demands change. Contemporary frameworks contend that flexibility and stability are not necessarily opposite ends of a single scale, but could instead reflect coordinated processes that regulate when to maintain a goal and when to update it \parencite{egner2023principles}. In everyday life, cognitive flexibility supports switching between rules, adapting to new information, and shifting attention between competing task sets \parencite{diamond2013executive, friedman2017unity}. When flexibility fails, behaviour becomes rigid, error-prone, or inefficient, especially in situations that require rapid reconfiguration of task goals and response mappings.

\paragraph{What is sleep and why is it a plausible source of variation?}

Although public awareness has risen of the importance of a good night's sleep, it is often taken for granted in everyday life. Most people recognize the immediate effects of poor sleep, such as reduced concentration or increased irritability. Beyond these familiar experiences, sleep also plays a central role in maintaining cognitive, emotional, and physiological functioning. Understanding what sleep is and how it is regulated is important for examining its relationship with cognition. 

Sleep is generally understood to be governed by the interaction of two core regulatory processes. The first is a homeostatic mechanism in which pressure for sleep gradually builds during wakefulness and dissipates once sleep occurs. The second is the circadian timing system, which organizes sleep and wakefulness across the approximately 24-hour day and helps align behaviour with environmental light–dark cycles \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Rather than being generated by a single brain region, sleep emerges from coordinated activity across large-scale neural networks. Transitions between sleep and wakefulness are regulated through interactions between sleep-promoting and arousal systems. Sleep itself is not uniform but consists of distinct stages, such as non-rapid eye movement and rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, each characterized by different patterns of neural oscillatory activity that are believed to support processes including synaptic plasticity and memory processing \parencite{adamantidis2019oscillating}. Sleep is also closely embedded within broader biological rhythms that regulate functions ranging from hormone release to metabolism. When this temporal organization is disrupted through sleep restriction, circadian misalignment, or fragmented sleep, the consequences can extend well beyond fatigue. Modern societal demands frequently challenge these biological systems, and accumulating evidence links insufficient or disrupted sleep to negative outcomes for both mental and physical health \parencite{foster2020sleep}. From a brain health perspective, sleep is increasingly understood as an active biological state that supports neural maintenance and long-term functioning. Theories of sleep highlight roles in synaptic regulation, metabolic clearance, and neural restoration, suggesting that sleep contributes to preserving brain integrity across the lifespan \parencite{fjell2025sleeppatterns}. One proposed mechanism involves the clearance of metabolic waste products. Experimental evidence shows that even a single night of sleep deprivation can increase $\beta$-Amyloid burden in regions such as the hippocampus and thalamus, indicating that sleep may facilitate the removal of potentially neurotoxic substances from the brain \parencite{Shokri-Kojori2018beta-amyloid}. Consistent with this view, disrupted sleep has also been linked to structural brain changes, including patterns of cortical atrophy associated with poorer cognitive outcomes \parencite{sexton2014sleepatrophy}. With these findings in mind sleep appears to be important both for immediate cognitive functioning and for long-term brain health.

Several different characteristics of sleep have turned out to be meaningful in research. Sleep duration is one of the most studied dimensions, and refers to the total time spent asleep. Cohort studies suggest a quadratic relationship between sleep duration and executive functioning, with both short and long sleep associated with poorer performance, and approximately seven hours linked to more optimal outcomes \parencite{tai2022sleepef, sen2023sleepef}. However, duration alone provides an incomplete picture. Sleep efficiency, typically defined as the proportion of time in bed spent asleep, reflects how consolidated sleep is. Continuity captures the degree to which sleep is fragmented by awakenings, while variability refers to night-to-night fluctuations in sleep patterns. Emerging evidence suggests that these qualitative aspects may be at least as important as total sleep time when considering cognitive consequences \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Sleep quality also appears to have broad implications for cognitive processes central to executive functioning. Experimental sleep deprivation often impairs sustained attention, slows reaction time, and reduces working memory capacity. Individuals who are sleep deprived also tend to rely more on habitual responses rather than goal-directed control, suggesting reduced cognitive flexibility \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. These effects are not limited to extreme deprivation. Poor sleep efficiency and fragmented sleep have been linked to worse executive function and increased risk of later cognitive decline, indicating that everyday variations in sleep can meaningfully influence cognitive performance \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Mood also appears to be sensitive to sleep disruption. Acute sleep loss has been associated with mood worsening, and greater increases in $\beta$-Amyloid following deprivation have been correlated with more negative mood states \parencite{Shokri-Kojori2018beta-amyloid}, whereas better sleep has been related to positive moods \parencite{alibasa2022switchingmood}. Abnormal sleep is closely tied to mental health conditions such as anxiety and depression \parencite{wilckens2020sleepmddswitch, bei2018sleepdepression, franzen2008sleepdepression, sadler2018insomniadepression}, with evidence suggesting a bidirectional relationship in which sleep problems both contribute to and arise from affective symptoms \parencite{alvaro2013sleepdepression, sivertsen2012bidirectional}. This overlap is reflected in common assessment tools, such as MINI and MADRS, where sleep disturbance is included as a core symptom of depression.

The relationship between sleep and cognition varies across individuals and across the lifespan. Executive functioning naturally declines with age, and sleep architecture undergoes parallel changes, including reduced efficiency and increased fragmentation \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. At the same time, growing evidence challenges the assumption that a single optimal sleep duration applies to everyone. Individual sleep needs appear to be flexible and dynamically related to cognitive functioning, suggesting that both trait-like differences and state-dependent fluctuations should be considered when evaluating sleep–cognition associations \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Night-to-night variability is common, also among healthy adults, and may carry cognitive consequences that are not captured by average sleep estimates. Discrepancies between subjective and objective sleep measures is another factor that points to the complexity of accurately characterizing sleep behaviour \parencite{sen2023sleepef}.

\section{The current study}
- superbrief summary
- research questions
- hypotheses
- quick methodology overview

The present thesis attempts to address these gaps by examining how objectively measured sleep patterns relate to cognitive flexibility and its neural correlates. Using data from the Mind the Sleep project, sleep is quantified over four weeks using actigraphy, allowing estimation of both habitual sleep level (e.g., mean sleep duration and efficiency) and sleep variability (e.g., night-to-night fluctuations in these measures). Cognitive flexibility is assessed with a task-switching paradigm performed during an EEG session, enabling analysis of both behavioural performance and FM-theta activity during task switching. I combine objective sleep assessment with behavioural and electrophysiological markers of cognitive control in this thesis, with the aim to gain a nuanced understanding of how sleep patterns relate to flexible cognition in adults.

The overarching research question is:

\begin{quote}
To what extent do habitual sleep level and sleep variability predict cognitive flexibility and frontal midline theta during a task-switching paradigm?
\end{quote}

Based on prior work linking sleep disturbances to impaired top-down control and altered control-related neural responses \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep,wilckens2014sleeppreparation}, I propose the following hypotheses. First, poorer habitual sleep (e.g., shorter average sleep duration and lower average sleep efficiency) is hypothesized to be associated with reduced cognitive flexibility, reflected in larger switch costs and/or stronger interference effects. Second, greater sleep variability is hypothesized to be associated with poorer cognitive flexibility, above and beyond habitual sleep level, reflecting less stable cognitive control capacity. Third, poorer habitual sleep and greater sleep variability are predicted to relate to altered FM-theta during task switching \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,depestele2023thetadriving}. Finally, if FM-theta reflects control recruitment during demanding trials, behavioural costs are expected to covary with theta markers of control engagement.


This thesis examines how multidimensional sleep patterns relate to flexible cognition and its neural signatures. I want to find out whether nightly fluctuations, sleep stability over time or both are relevant for cognitive control in everyday conditions.

\subsection{Ethical approval}
The project was approved by the Regional Committees for Medical and Health Research Ethics (REK \#197760).

\subsection{Data management}
In the Mind the Sleep project we collected information about health which is classified as sensitive data (Norwegian: særlige kategorier av personopplysninger). All data files generated as a part of MtS (i.e. EEG, MR, task performance, questionnaires, actigraphy) are stored on TSD. 

\subsection{Study design}
The study was a within subjects-design with 3 sessions separated by 2 weeks. Session 1 comprised MINI, questionnaire A, questionnaire B and receiving overall instructions regarding the study. Participants received their actigraphy watch at this time point and sleep diary. Hence, sleep data was collected over the timespan of four weeks. Session two and three were identical and comprised an EEG recording during a resting-state, Stop-signal, Switch number-letter, Stroop, and N-back task. In addition, participants filled out questionnaire A. They had the option to either do the questionnaire A at the lab or at home depending on their schedule demands. Questionnaire B was sent out to participants on a daily basis from session one onwards, hence, over the timespan of four weeks.

\paragraph{Sample}
Data collection is ongoing. The project includes adult participants (18-65 years), both with and without symptoms of depression. All participants are right-handed, speak Norwegian, and have normal or corrected-to-normal vision.
Participants (N = 50) are recruited through online advertisements on Instagram and Facebook, and screened for depression via phone using the MADRS depression screening tool. Eligible participants attend an initial 1.5-hour session including clinical interview (MINI) and baseline questionnaires. Participants are then equipped with actigraphy devices and instructed in daily sleep diary completion. They are also given daily questionnaires through e-mail. At two-week intervals, participants complete EEG sessions with executive function assessments (stop signal, task-switching, Stroop, and n-back tasks) and repeated questionnaire measures.
\paragraph{Exclusion Criteria}
Certain psychological and neurological disorders, sleep disturbances, and particular medications were grounds for exclusion.
Exclusion criteria: Schizophrenia, bipolar disorder or personality disorders (like borderline or dyssocial personality disorder), parkinsons, dementia, epilepsy, stroke, traumatic head injury. Psychotic disorders (current or in the past). Using medication like anticholinergic or antipsychotic medicine. Antiepileptica, tricyclic antidepressants or sleep medicine like nefazodone. Benzodiazepines. Substance abuse. 
We screen participants with MADRS in the initial phone interview. If they have a score over 20 they are excluded unless they are in contact with health care for their mental health.

\subsection{EEG}
Each EEG session contained a resting state recording (5 minutes with eyes open, 5 minutes with eyes closed) and four tasks in the following order: stop signal task (for 20 minutes), task-switching task (15 minutes), Stroop task (12 minutes), and the n-back task (25 minutes). \paragraph{Resting state}
Each EEG session contained 2 x 5 minutes resting state recordings, first with their eyes open and then with their eyes closed. Participants were told that we wanted to measure their brain activity during rest. To do this, they should just sit still, rest their gaze at the fixation cross on the screen, and let their thoughts wander. They were explicitly told to try to avoid movement, such as tapping their fingers or shaking their legs, and to try to avoid concrete time passing activities, such as counting.  


\paragraph{Task-Switching Paradigm}
In the task-switching task, participants were presented with a pair of one letter and one number on the screen for 1200 ms. The font colour of the pair was either blue or yellow. If the font colour was blue, then participants were instructed to report, by button press, if the number was even (left button) or odd (right button). If the font colour was yellow however, then participants were instructed to report, by button press, if the letter was a vowel (left button) or a consonant (right button). Prior to the presentation of the pair, a fixation cross was presented on the screen for a duration between 1300 and 1700ms.

In one-third of the trials, the font colour switched from the colour in the previous trial: the switch trials. In the remaining two-thirds of the trials, the font colour was the same as the previous trial: the repeat trials. The task consisted of four blocks, with each block consisting of 60 trials. Participants had 15 trials for practicing before the experiment began. In total, the task consisted of 240 trials, adding up to a total duration of approximately 15 minutes.

\paragraph{Feature Extraction from Actigraphy}
The derived sleep parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TST (Total Sleep Time)} - Estimated total duration of sleep, excluding awakenings. Greater total sleep time generally indicates better sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{SOL (Sleep Onset Latency)} - The time taken to fall asleep. Longer latency suggests poorer sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{WASO (Wake After Sleep Onset)} - Total time spent awake after initially falling asleep. Greater WASO reflects more fragmented sleep and thus lower sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{SE (Sleep Efficiency)} - Calculated as TST divided by time in bed, multiplied by 100\%. Values above 85–90\% are typically considered indicative of good sleep efficiency.
    \item \textbf{Number of awakenings} - The number of awakenings detected during the night. A higher number indicates poorer sleep continuity.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations of actigraphy} There are some limitations related to the use of actigraphy for sleep measurement. Polysomnography (PSG) remains the gold standard for assessing sleep because it provides a direct physiological measurement of sleep stages through simultaneous recording of brain activity (EEG), eye movements (EOG), and muscle tone (EMG), allowing precise differentiation between sleep and wakefulness (ADD A REFERENCE). PSG requires an extensive set-up, which makes it more suitable for lab studies. Wrist actigraphy is a practical and validated alternative for field studies where we are interested in habitual sleep. In the present study, only the accelerometer function of the ActiGraph devices was used, with all other sensors disabled to preserve battery life and participants’ privacy. Because actigraphy infers sleep and wake from movement alone, it may misclassify periods of quiet wakefulness as sleep, and can underestimate wake after sleep onset (WASO) compared with PSG (SOURCES, FOR GOD'S SAKE!!!). To ensure accuracy, actigraphy data were cross-checked against participants’ sleep diaries. Any apparent sleep periods not confirmed in the diary were excluded from analyses. Sources of errors here could be when participants take the actigraph off and leave it on the counter for periods of the day. The stillness will sometimes be registered as sleep.
