\chapter{Introduction}

Sleep is a fundamental biological process that supports cognitive and emotional functioning. Even in healthy adults, sleep duration and quality will differ across individuals and across time, shaped by circadian rhythms, lifestyle factors, and environmental demands \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Habitual sleep patterns may reflect relatively stable individual differences, whereas nightly deviations can reflect transient changes in arousal, fatigue, and regulatory capacity \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Understanding how these different aspects of sleep relate to cognition is important because everyday performance often depends on long-term sleep habits as well as sleep quality in the days leading up to a demanding situation.

There is an extensive body of research linking sleep and executive function, where two conceptual gaps are relevant for the present thesis. First, it is unclear how robust the effects of sleep on cognitive control are, and whether findings from controlled sleep deprivation studies extend to more naturalistic sleep. Much of the literature relies on acute manipulations of sleep loss, and it remains uncertain to what extent these findings generalize beyond such settings. In addition, cognitive control is not a unitary construct, and different components, such as stability and flexibility, may be affected in different ways. It is therefore unclear whether sleep has similar or distinct effects across these aspects of control. This also raises the question of whether different aspects of sleep, such as continuity and variability, modulate these components in the same way. Second, even when studies use objective and ecologically valid measures of sleep, fewer distinguish between habitual sleep level, that is, how a person typically sleeps over days or weeks, and sleep variability, that is, how much sleep fluctuates from night to night. These dimensions may have different cognitive correlates. Habitual sleep may reflect more stable characteristics, whereas variability may capture instability in sleep timing and quality that is relevant for cognitive control \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. This distinction is therefore important for understanding whether stable sleep patterns and night-to-night fluctuations relate differently to cognitive control.



\section{Stability and flexibility}

We rely on executive functions in everyday life, and especially when navigating novel or demanding situations, such as planning tasks or solving problems creatively \parencite{diamond2013executive, frith2021creativity}. When our executive functions fail, we can experience odd glitches like putting a used plate in the fridge instead of in the dishwasher, or being distracted by our phone during a meeting. Lapses in attention, difficulties updating behaviour, or an inability to suppress irrelevant information can also have more serious consequences, like impairing decision-making in contexts like academic performance, clinical judgement or traffic safety. Understanding the mechanisms that support flexible, goal-directed behaviour is a central challenge in cognitive neuroscience. Executive functions are often described as higher-order processes that enable us to regulate thoughts and actions in accordance with internal goals. A highly influential account is the unity and diversity framework proposed by \textcite{friedman2017unity}, which reconciles two seemingly competing views: that executive functions reflect a common control capacity, and that they consist of partially separable components. According to this model, tasks requiring inhibition, shifting, and updating share variance because they draw on a domain-general ability to actively maintain goals and bias processing toward task-relevant information. At the same time, each component captures additional processes that are not fully reducible to this shared control factor. Shifting tasks, for example, involve mechanisms related specifically to disengaging from one task set and activating another. This perspective implies that cognitive control can not be completely explained as a single unified faculty, nor is it a collection of entirely independent skills. Instead, executive functions can be seen as coordinated processes that operate together to support adaptive behaviour. 

A central component of cognitive control is cognitive flexibility, the ability to adapt behaviour when goals or task demands change. These processes have traditionally been treated as opposing ends of a single dimension, implying that increased flexibility comes at the cost of reduced stability. However, contemporary frameworks contend that flexibility and stability are not necessarily opposite ends of a single scale, but could instead reflect coordinated processes that regulate when to maintain a goal and when to update it \parencite{egner2023principles, nack2023metacognition}. In everyday life, cognitive flexibility supports switching between rules, adapting to new information, and shifting attention between competing task sets \parencite{diamond2013executive, friedman2017unity}. When flexibility fails, behaviour becomes rigid, error-prone, or inefficient, especially in situations that require rapid reconfiguration of task goals and response mappings. Stability refers to maintaining task-relevant representations and shielding them from distraction (like chopping vegetables for dinner), whereas flexibility refers to updating those representations when task demands change (stopping the pot from boiling over). Mechanistically, the coordination of set shifting has been described in terms of biasing and gating processes in working memory. Biasing prioritizes currently relevant representations, while gating determines when new information is allowed to update the current task set. Drawing on pharmacological neuroimaging studies, \cite{cools2019gatingmechanisms} proposes that prefrontal dopamine supports the stable maintenance of task representations, whereas striatal dopamine enables flexible updating through different gating mechanisms. Engaging these control processes seems to be effortful and associated with subjective cost, and individuals tend to avoid recruiting control unless it is required or rewarded \parencite{kool2010cognitivecost, cools2016cognitivecost}. This applies to control states more generally, suggesting that variability in control also can reflect changes in motivation and resource availability rather than just the underlying capacity. These neural correlates do not necessarily lend more support to a unidimensional or dual dimension framework of set shifting.

\cite{nack2023metacognition} make a case for a dual-dimensional view of set shifting within a framework of metacontrol, defined as context-dependent regulation of cognitive control. Within this framework, flexibility and stability are conceptualized as metacontrol states instead of fixed capacities. They argue that manipulations of flexibility within participants do not consistently produce corresponding changes in stability. For example, increasing the frequency of task switches reliably reduces switch costs, indicating increased flexibility, but does not necessarily increase congruency effects, which would be expected if stability were reduced \parencite{geddert2022tradeoff}. In some cases, individuals adapt to increased demands for switching without showing any change in interference from irrelevant information. Similar patterns are observed when flexibility is manipulated through training or affect \parencite{zhao2020switchtraining}. Improvements in switching performance do not appear to come at the cost of reduced stability. Increasing flexibility does not make people more susceptible to interference from irrelevant information \parencite{sali2021flexibleattention}. Together, these findings suggest that flexibility can be upregulated without a corresponding decrease in stability, which is difficult to reconcile with a strict trade-off account.
Flexibility and stability do not show the negative relationship predicted by a unidimensional framework in between-participant studies either. If the two processes were strictly opposed, individuals with higher flexibility should consistently show lower stability. Instead, individual differences often affect one component without systematically influencing the other. For instance, factors such as training, bilingual experience, or emotion regulation can improve switching performance without altering interference effects \parencite{christoeffels2015bilingualflexible, mcrae2012reappraisalcontrol}. Conversely, some factors affect stability without changing flexibility. When asked, participants will often say they are either good or bad at both shifting attention and keeping focus, and they rarely see these skills as needing to be traded off against each other \parencite{derryberry2002anxiouscontrol}. Factor analyses have also shown that stability and flexibility load onto two factors and not one, meaning that they seem to reflect partly independent processes \parencite{bejjani2022latentswitch, chiorri2017factoranalysis}.

Evidence for this dissociation is also found at the neural level. Neuropsychological studies show double dissociations, where damage to different brain regions selectively impairs either flexibility or stability. For example, lesions to the superior medial frontal cortex are associated with increased perseverative errors, indicating impaired flexibility, whereas lesions to the inferior medial frontal cortex are associated with increased set-loss errors, indicating impaired stability \parencite{stuss2000doubledissociation}. A study by \cite{denouden2013dissociablelearning} showed dissociable effects of dopaminergic and serotonergic systems on reversal learning. Variation in serotonin transporter function selectively influenced lose-shift behaviour, reflecting sensitivity to negative feedback. Variation in dopamine transporter function influenced perseveration after reversal, reflecting the tendency to rely on previously learned associations. Since these effects did not overlap, updating based on immediate feedback and the persistence of prior learning could be supported by partly separable mechanisms. Both within-individual manipulations and between-individual differences, as well as dissociations in neural correlates, seem to suggest that flexibility and stability are not necessarily locked in a trade-off. Instead, they can vary independently depending on task demands, context, and individual characteristics. This distinction is important for the present thesis, as it implies that different aspects of cognitive control may be differentially sensitive to variations in sleep, rather than being affected uniformly.


\section{Measuring stability and flexibility}

When operationalizing cognitive stability and flexibility in task-switching paradigms, we try to bridge the gap between underlying constructs and their behavioural indicators. In everyday life, executive functions like shielding focus or shifting between tasks are complex, integrated, and context-dependent. These processes involve multiple interacting components, including goal maintenance, attentional selection, working memory updating, and response selection. In order to study executive functions in the lab, we need to reduce the complexity by isolating specific components of behaviour. \textcite{rogers1995taskswitch} and \textcite{kiesel2010review} both highlight that defining a general rule of what constitutes a task set is difficult. Experiments will typically use something like categorizing letters or numbers, reading words, naming colours or objects. Participants get a specified rule to follow, if stimulus X then action Y. Often the stimulus is bivalent, meaning that it can elicit two different responses depending on the active set rule. Cognitive flexibility is operationalized as the ability to update or reconfigure task sets, indexed by switch costs, whereas cognitive stability is operationalized as the ability to maintain a task set in the presence of interference, indexed by congruency effects. These measures provide tractable and quantifiable proxies for otherwise multifaceted processes. However, this reduction comes at a cost. Because task performance reflects the combined contribution of several cognitive processes, these measures are not process-pure. For example, switch costs may reflect task-set reconfiguration, but it can also reflect interference from previous trials or differences in task preparation. Congruency effects can reflect both attentional control and stimulus–response competition \parencite{egner2023principles}. This issue, often referred to as task impurity, complicates the interpretation of behavioural indices as direct measures of stability and flexibility. In addition, the simplified and repetitive nature of laboratory tasks limits their ecological validity. Real-world cognitive control involves dynamically changing goals, richer contextual information, and self-initiated behaviour, which are only partially captured by controlled experimental paradigms. As such, task-switching measures should be understood as constrained operationalizations of broader executive functions rather than direct reflections of everyday cognitive control.

Task-switching paradigms are often used to investigate cognitive flexibility because they capture the ability to disengage from one task set and configure another in response to changing demands \parencite{monsell2003switching,kiesel2010review, rogers1995taskswitch, egner2023principles}. In the task switching paradigms like the one used in this thesis, participants alternate between simple classification rules. We then quantify flexibility using behavioural measures such as  accuracy and reaction times. Typically, participants will produce slower and less accurate responses on rule switching trials than on repetition trials. The switch cost is the difference in response time and/or accuracy between switch trials and repeat trials. Slower and more error-prone responses on switch trials are interpreted as reflecting the cognitive demands associated with updating task sets, including reconfiguration processes and interference from the previously relevant rule. Smaller switch costs are taken to indicate greater cognitive flexibility, or increased “switch readiness” \parencite{egner2023principles}.
Some task-switching designs also manipulate stimulus congruency, to investigate stability. Congruent stimuli afford the same response across task rules, whereas incongruent stimuli activate competing response mappings. That is, if a task is congruent it will elicit the same response, like a left button press, for both task rules. If the stimuli is incongruent it will elicit different responses for the different task rules, like a left button click for one rule and a right button click for the other. Congruency effects provide an additional measure of interference control to the switch cost in the set shifting task. There are some claims that interference is often amplified on switch trials, suggesting that the need to update task sets may temporarily increase susceptibility to competing stimulus–response associations \parencite{kiesel2010review}. If flexibility and stability load onto two different factors instead of one, we would expect that they are not inherently constrained by each other.

Task-switching paradigms can also combine switching and conflict manipulations. In these combined paradigms, congruent stimuli afford the same response across task rules, whereas incongruent stimuli activate competing response mappings. This allows for the simultaneous assessment of flexibility (via switch costs) and stability (via congruency effects) within the same task. Across these paradigms, behavioral performance is typically indexed using response times (RTs) and accuracy rates, from which switch costs and congruency effects are derived. Both switch costs and congruency effects are relative measures that reflect differences between conditions rather than absolute performance. Together, these measures provide complementary indices of the efficiency with which individuals update task sets and protect them from interference.


\section{Electrophysiological correlates}

When we measure brain activity we work from certain assumptions about brain anatomy and physiological mechanisms. Electroencephalogram (EEG) is a non-invasive and cost-effective measure of electrical activity in the brain. The brain is composed of grey and white matter, which are broadly organized in similar ways across individuals, although the brain is highly plastic and can adapt to structural variation. Grey matter contains densely packed neuronal cell bodies, whereas white matter primarily consists of myelinated axons that facilitate efficient communication between regions by increasing conduction speed. Neural communication involves both electrical and chemical processes. Within a neuron, electrical signals are propagated as action potentials, which arise from changes in membrane potential due to the movement of ions across the cell membrane. At rest, neurons have a membrane potential of approximately $-70$ mV, maintained by differences in ion concentrations, with higher concentrations of potassium inside the cell and sodium outside. When an action potential is generated, voltage-gated ion channels open, allowing sodium ions to enter the cell, leading to rapid depolarization, followed by repolarization as potassium exits the cell \parencite[Chapter 2]{gazzaniga2025principles}. EEG does not measure individual action potentials, but reflects the summed postsynaptic potentials of large populations of active neurons in the cortex. These signals propagate through brain tissue, cerebrospinal fluid, skull, and scalp before being detected by electrodes on the scalp. As a result, EEG has high temporal resolution but relatively low spatial resolution. This allows us to track the timing of neural processes with millisecond precision, even though the exact spatial sources of the signals are less precisely localized \parencite[Chapter 3]{gazzaniga2025principles}. EEG is commonly used to investigate cognitive conflict and set shifting \parencite{luck2014erp, cohen2013thetaconflict, cavanagh2015metatheta, cavanagh2014theta, depestele2023thetadriving}.

At the neural level, cognitive flexibility is supported by a distributed frontoparietal control network involving medial and lateral prefrontal regions as well as posterior parietal cortex \parencite{kiesel2010review}. These regions are implicated in goal maintenance, attentional allocation, and the resolution of interference, all of which are required when behaviour must be reconfigured in response to changing task demands. Successful set shifting depends on the ability to represent multiple task rules, as well as mechanisms that monitor conflict and signal the need for increased control \parencite{botvinick2001conflict}. Conflict processing has traditionally been studied using event-related potentials (ERPs), which reflect phase-locked neural responses to stimulus onset. One well-established component is the N2, a negative deflection peaking approximately 200–300 ms after stimulus onset. The N2 component is thought to index conflict detection processes occurring between stimulus evaluation and response execution. Conflict related neural activity can also be captured by increases in midfrontal theta-band power (FM-theta, 4–8 Hz), which largely reflect non-phase-locked oscillatory dynamics. Event-related oscillatory activity can be distinguished into evoked and induced components. Evoked activity is phase-locked to stimulus onset and therefore preserved in the averaged ERP, whereas induced activity varies in phase across trials and largely cancels out during averaging \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}. As a result, ERP analyses primarily reflect phase-locked responses and may underestimate oscillatory dynamics that are not strictly time-locked. Time–frequency analysis is an alternative that is applied at the single-trial level prior to averaging, allowing both evoked and induced activity to be captured in measures of total activity, often referred to as event-related spectral perturbation (ERSP).
Time–frequency analyses have shown that theta-band power can persist after removal of the ERP, suggesting partially distinct neural processes \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}, with theta activity being more closely linked to behavioral performance \parencite{cohen2013thetaconflict}.

Frontal midline theta (FM-theta) is a non-phase-locked rhythmic power change typically observed over medial frontal scalp sites. Converging evidence from EEG and intracranial recordings suggests that FM-theta is primarily generated in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and adjacent medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), regions implicated in performance monitoring and the dynamic allocation of control \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,depestele2023thetadriving}. FM-theta is thought to index a domain-general control signal that increases when ongoing behaviour requires adjustment. Accordingly, FM-theta power is enhanced under conditions that place elevated demands on control, including response conflict, error commission, and task switching. Switch trials and incongruent trials often elicit stronger theta activity than low-conflict conditions, suggesting that medial frontal systems register the need for increased control when competing task sets or response tendencies are activated \parencite{cohen2013thetaconflict, cavanagh2014theta}. In this sense, FM-theta can be understood as a neural signature of control engagement during flexible behaviour.


\section{Stable ability or context-sensitive?}

Executive functions (EF) have been conceptualized both as stable cognitive capacities and as context-sensitive control processes that vary across situations. From a trait-perspective, EF is conceptualized as a relatively stable capacity that supports goal-directed behaviour across domains. This view is supported by the unity–diversity framework proposed by \textcite{friedman2017unity}. Using latent variable approaches, they show that the executive functions inhibition, updating, and shifting are both separable and correlated, reflecting both a common underlying factor as well as domain-specific components. In their latent variable analysis, the letter–number switching task loads moderately on the shifting factor ($\lambda \approx$.66), indicating that performance on this task partly reflects an underlying trait-like shifting ability. However, a considerable proportion of the variance remains task-specific ($\approx$ .56), meaning that performance is also influenced by processes that are unique to the task itself. Each individual task in the factor analysis, like the letter–number switching task, reflects a mixture of three things. Part of the performance reflects an underlying ability we are interested in, such as shifting. Another part reflects factors that are specific to that task, such as perceptual demands, strategies and momentary attention. The third part reflects residual noise. In this way, no single task is a pure measure of an executive function. When we use a latent variable approach, we look at how performance on several different tasks relates to each other. If a person performs well on one shifting task, they also tend to perform well on other shifting tasks. The part of performance that is consistent across these tasks is treated as shared variance. This shared variance is interpreted as reflecting the underlying executive function, because it is the component that generalizes across different task formats. We can aggregate the tasks by statistically extracting what multiple tasks have in common, rather than by simply averaging scores. By isolating this shared component across many tasks, we can reduce the influence of task-specific noise. What remains is a latent factor, such as the Common EF  \textcite{friedman2017unity} propose, which is more stable over time and across contexts than performance on any single one task. When the common variance across tasks is isolated, a stable underlying control capacity becomes visible. Individual tasks on their own are much noisier and more influenced by context. Even paradigmatic measures of control, such as the stop-signal task, exhibit low test–retest reliability, with substantial variation across sessions that cannot be explained by measurement noise alone. This pattern suggests that commonly used behavioural indices of control are strongly influenced by situational factors and may not reflect stable traits to the extent often assumed \parencite{thunberg2024unreliability}. The unity-diversity framework implies that variability in task performance does not necessarily reflect changes in the underlying executive function itself.  Fluctuations in performance can instead arise from state-dependent factors that influence how control is deployed in a given context. These influences are not captured by the latent factor itself, but remain in the task-specific and residual variance.

Further support for the trait view comes from behavioural genetics. Executive functions are found to be highly heritable at the latent level and influenced by polygenic factors, indicating that stable biological differences contribute to variation in control abilities \parencite{friedman2011genetic}. Longitudinal studies show that early differences in self-control predict later outcomes across domains such as academic achievement, health, and social functioning. The classic example is the marshmallow test, where delay of gratification in early childhood has been linked to life outcomes decades later \parencite{mischel1988marshmallow}. These findings have been widely interpreted as evidence that executive control reflects a stable, domain-general capacity. Even though executive functions show properties such as heritability, stability over time and predictive validity, the picture may be more nuanced. For example, in strategic decision-making, participants need to determine how much evidence to accumulate before committing to a response, like when deciding whether dots on a screen are moving to the left or right. \textcite{bogacz2010speedaccuracy} describe this process as the gradual accumulation of noisy evidence toward a decision threshold. The process starts from a given starting point, which can be biased toward one of the response options, and continues until the accumulated evidence reaches a threshold that triggers a response. A higher threshold requires more evidence, leading to slower but more accurate decisions, whereas a lower threshold allows for faster but more error-prone responses. Individuals tend to show relatively consistent response tendencies, reflected in stable starting points or general levels of caution. At the same time, these parameters can be adjusted depending on task demands, allowing individuals to trade speed for accuracy when needed. The starting point can be understood as reflecting a stable response bias, whereas changes in threshold reflect flexible control. This suggests that behaviour is shaped both by relatively stable biases and by flexible, context-dependent adjustments that are implemented online in response to current goals.

As for the predictive validity of EF traits, reinterpretations of classic findings challenge the notion that early behaviour reflects intrinsic, stable self-control capacity. Reanalyses of the marshmallow test indicate that its predictive validity may be better explained by social and environmental factors, such as trust and socioeconomic context, rather than stable individual differences in willpower. In this view, children's willingness to delay gratification reflects adaptive responses to their environment rather than fixed control abilities \parencite{michaelson2020marshmallow}. Contemporary models of cognitive control emphasize its adaptive and dynamic nature. Rather than reflecting a fixed capacity, control is thought to be continuously adjusted based on task demands, recent experience, and expected outcomes. Neurocognitive accounts described in \textcite{cavanagh2015metatheta}'s metareview suggest that frontal midline theta signals index the need for control and coordinate its implementation across brain networks, supporting a view of control as an online, context-sensitive process rather than a static trait. Metacognitive accounts further reinforce the state-based perspective by emphasizing that individuals monitor their own performance and allocate control accordingly. Control is costly and selectively deployed, increasing in situations of conflict, uncertainty, or error, and decreasing when demands are low. This selective allocation implies that variability in performance may reflect differences in control engagement rather than differences in underlying capacity \parencite{cavanagh2015metatheta}. Similarly, frameworks such as the dual mechanisms of control and adaptive control theories describe control as flexibly shifting between modes depending on situational demands \parencite{egner2023principles, nack2023metacognition, cavanagh2014theta}.

Taken together, these findings suggest that while stable individual differences in executive function clearly exist, they do not fully account for observed behaviour. Executive control appears to emerge from an interaction between relatively stable predispositions and dynamically changing contextual influences. In this sense, cognitive control can be studied as a flexible system that adapts to current demands, internal states, and environmental constraints.

\paragraph{What is sleep and why is it a plausible source of variation?}

 Sleep disturbances are associated with impairments in executive functions \parencite{sen2023sleepef,tai2022sleepef, slama2018sleepswitching, wilckens2020sleepmddswitch, wilckens2014sleeppreparation, fjell2024sleep, kusztor2019differentialsleep, wilckens2016sleepcognitionage, basner2013sleepcognition, couyoumdjian2010switchingsleep}. The relationship between sleep and executive functioning appears to be selective in the sense that experimental sleep deprivation appears to impair some cognitive operations while sparing others \parencite{killgore2010sleepcognition}. Studies show that top-down control processes are more strongly distrupted than relatively automatic or stimulus-driven processes \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep}. This pattern suggests that sleep may be more relevant for cognitive control demands that require maintaining and updating task goals, resolving interference, and flexibly adjusting behavior to changing task requirements.


Although public awareness has risen of the importance of a good night's sleep, it is often taken for granted in everyday life. Most people recognize the immediate effects of poor sleep, such as reduced concentration or increased irritability. Beyond these familiar experiences, sleep also plays a central role in maintaining cognitive, emotional, and physiological functioning. Understanding what sleep is and how it is regulated is important for examining its relationship with cognition. 

Sleep is generally understood to be governed by the interaction of two core regulatory processes. The first is a homeostatic mechanism in which pressure for sleep gradually builds during wakefulness and dissipates once sleep occurs. The second is the circadian timing system, which organizes sleep and wakefulness across the approximately 24-hour day and helps align behaviour with environmental light–dark cycles \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Rather than being generated by a single brain region, sleep emerges from coordinated activity across large-scale neural networks. Transitions between sleep and wakefulness are regulated through interactions between sleep-promoting and arousal systems. Sleep itself is not uniform but consists of distinct stages, such as non-rapid eye movement and rapid eye movement (REM) sleep, each characterized by different patterns of neural oscillatory activity that are believed to support processes including synaptic plasticity and memory processing \parencite{adamantidis2019oscillating}. Sleep is also closely embedded within broader biological rhythms that regulate functions ranging from hormone release to metabolism. When this temporal organization is disrupted through sleep restriction, circadian misalignment, or fragmented sleep, the consequences can extend well beyond fatigue. Modern societal demands frequently challenge these biological systems, and accumulating evidence links insufficient or disrupted sleep to negative outcomes for both mental and physical health \parencite{foster2020sleep}. From a brain health perspective, sleep is increasingly understood as an active biological state that supports neural maintenance and long-term functioning. Theories of sleep highlight roles in synaptic regulation, metabolic clearance, and neural restoration, suggesting that sleep contributes to preserving brain integrity across the lifespan \parencite{fjell2025sleeppatterns}. One proposed mechanism involves the clearance of metabolic waste products. Experimental evidence shows that even a single night of sleep deprivation can increase $\beta$-Amyloid burden in regions such as the hippocampus and thalamus, indicating that sleep may facilitate the removal of potentially neurotoxic substances from the brain \parencite{Shokri-Kojori2018beta-amyloid}. Consistent with this view, disrupted sleep has also been linked to structural brain changes, including patterns of cortical atrophy associated with poorer cognitive outcomes \parencite{sexton2014sleepatrophy}. With these findings in mind sleep appears to be important both for immediate cognitive functioning and for long-term brain health.

Several different characteristics of sleep have turned out to be meaningful in research. Sleep duration is one of the most studied dimensions, and refers to the total time spent asleep. Cohort studies suggest a quadratic relationship between sleep duration and executive functioning, with both short and long sleep associated with poorer performance, and approximately seven hours linked to more optimal outcomes \parencite{tai2022sleepef, sen2023sleepef}. However, duration alone provides an incomplete picture. Sleep efficiency, typically defined as the proportion of time in bed spent asleep, reflects how consolidated sleep is. Continuity captures the degree to which sleep is fragmented by awakenings, while variability refers to night-to-night fluctuations in sleep patterns. Emerging evidence suggests that these qualitative aspects may be at least as important as total sleep time when considering cognitive consequences \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Sleep quality also appears to have broad implications for cognitive processes central to executive functioning. Experimental sleep deprivation often impairs sustained attention, slows reaction time, and reduces working memory capacity. Individuals who are sleep deprived also tend to rely more on habitual responses rather than goal-directed control, suggesting reduced cognitive flexibility \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. These effects are not limited to extreme deprivation. Poor sleep efficiency and fragmented sleep have been linked to worse executive function and increased risk of later cognitive decline, indicating that everyday variations in sleep can meaningfully influence cognitive performance \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Mood also appears to be sensitive to sleep disruption. Acute sleep loss has been associated with mood worsening, and greater increases in $\beta$-Amyloid following deprivation have been correlated with more negative mood states \parencite{Shokri-Kojori2018beta-amyloid}, whereas better sleep has been related to positive moods \parencite{alibasa2022switchingmood}. Abnormal sleep is closely tied to mental health conditions such as anxiety and depression \parencite{wilckens2020sleepmddswitch, bei2018sleepdepression, franzen2008sleepdepression, sadler2018insomniadepression}, with evidence suggesting a bidirectional relationship in which sleep problems both contribute to and arise from affective symptoms \parencite{alvaro2013sleepdepression, sivertsen2012bidirectional}. This overlap is reflected in common assessment tools, such as MINI and MADRS, where sleep disturbance is included as a core symptom of depression.

The relationship between sleep and cognition varies across individuals and across the lifespan. Executive functioning naturally declines with age, and sleep architecture undergoes parallel changes, including reduced efficiency and increased fragmentation \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. At the same time, growing evidence challenges the assumption that a single optimal sleep duration applies to everyone. Individual sleep needs appear to be flexible and dynamically related to cognitive functioning, suggesting that both trait-like differences and state-dependent fluctuations should be considered when evaluating sleep–cognition associations \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Night-to-night variability is common, also among healthy adults, and may carry cognitive consequences that are not captured by average sleep estimates. Discrepancies between subjective and objective sleep measures is another factor that points to the complexity of accurately characterizing sleep behaviour \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. 

Research indicates that this control-related neural activity is sensitive to sleep. Experimental sleep deprivation has been associated with diminished theta responses during cognitively demanding tasks, alongside behavioural impairments in executive functioning \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep,wilckens2014sleeppreparation,wilckens2020sleepmddswitch}. Findings like this support the view that insufficient or disrupted sleep may compromise the neural systems responsible for monitoring performance and recruiting control. Altogether, these observations motivate the use of FM-theta as a neural outcome in the present thesis. If sleep contributes to the stability of cognitive control, individual differences in habitual sleep and sleep variability may be reflected both in behavioural performance and in the magnitude of control-related theta activity. FM-theta may therefore function as a state-sensitive neural marker linking multidimensional sleep patterns to the efficiency with which control is engaged during task switching.

\paragraph{Task-Switching Paradigm}
In the task-switching task, participants were presented with a pair of one letter and one number on the screen for 1200 ms. The font colour of the pair was either blue or yellow. If the font colour was blue, then participants were instructed to report, by button press, if the number was even (left button) or odd (right button). Prior to the presentation of the pair, a fixation cross was presented on the screen for a duration between 1300 and 1700ms.

In the remaining two-thirds of the trials, the font colour was the same as the previous trial: the repeat trials. The task consisted of four blocks, with each block consisting of 60 trials. \paragraph{Feature Extraction from Sleep Diaries}
The derived sleep parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TST (Total Sleep Time)} - Estimated total duration of sleep, excluding awakenings. Greater total sleep time generally indicates better sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{SOL (Sleep Onset Latency)} - The time taken to fall asleep. Longer latency suggests poorer sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{WASO (Wake After Sleep Onset)} - Total time spent awake after initially falling asleep. Greater WASO reflects more fragmented sleep and thus lower sleep quality.
    \item \textbf{SE (Sleep Efficiency)} - Calculated as TST divided by time in bed, multiplied by 100\%. Values above 85–90\% are typically considered indicative of good sleep efficiency.
    \item \textbf{Number of awakenings} - The number of awakenings detected during the night. A higher number indicates poorer sleep continuity.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations of actigraphy} There are some limitations related to the use of actigraphy for sleep measurement. Polysomnography (PSG) remains the gold standard for assessing sleep because it provides a direct physiological measurement of sleep stages through simultaneous recording of brain activity (EEG), eye movements (EOG), and muscle tone (EMG), allowing precise differentiation between sleep and wakefulness (ADD A REFERENCE). PSG requires an extensive set-up, which makes it more suitable for lab studies. Wrist actigraphy is a practical and validated alternative for field studies where we are interested in habitual sleep. In the present study, only the accelerometer function of the ActiGraph devices was used, with all other sensors disabled to preserve battery life and participants’ privacy. Because actigraphy infers sleep and wake from movement alone, it may misclassify periods of quiet wakefulness as sleep, and can underestimate wake after sleep onset (WASO) compared with PSG (SOURCES, FOR GOD'S SAKE!!!). To ensure accuracy, actigraphy data were cross-checked against participants’ sleep diaries. Any apparent sleep periods not confirmed in the diary were excluded from analyses. Sources of errors here could be when participants take the actigraph off and leave it on the counter for periods of the day. The stillness will sometimes be registered as sleep.


\section{Statistical Analysis}

Statistical analyses were conducted to examine behavioural performance, neural activity, and their associations with sleep. Behavioural and EEG data were analysed using repeated-measures analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with the within-subject factors switch (switch vs. repeat) and congruency (congruent vs. incongruent). A 2×2 repeated-measures ANOVA tests whether mean differences between the four conditions (defined by two factors with two levels each) are larger than expected by chance, while accounting for within-participant variability.

Behavioural performance was assessed using reaction times, accuracy, and omission rates. Reaction time data were first extracted at the trial level for each participant. Trials were classified according to switch (switch vs. repeat) and congruency (congruent vs. incongruent). For each participant, reaction times were then averaged separately within each of the four resulting conditions (repeat–congruent, repeat–incongruent, switch–congruent, switch–incongruent). These condition-specific mean reaction times were used as input to the repeated-measures ANOVA. Omission trials were excluded from reaction time analyses.

Habitual sleep was operationalised as the participant’s mean sleep across the recording period, and variability was indexed using the within-participant standard deviation. Associations between habitual sleep measures and behavioural and neural outcomes were assessed using linear regression models. To examine short-term effects of sleep, night-to-night deviations were calculated by centring each night’s sleep relative to the participant’s own mean. These deviation scores were entered as predictors in linear mixed-effects models with random intercepts for participants, allowing assessment of within-participant relationships between sleep and performance. All analyses were conducted using an alpha level of .05.

\paragraph{Preprocessing} I applied a low pass filter (40Hz), resampled to 500Hz, then a high pass filter (0.1Hz) and removed EMG channels. I then mapped electrode names and added FCz as the empty reference eletrode, before re-referencing to the average of the earlobes. I created an event list and renamed the triggers for epoching (using a different script). Then I ran an independent component analysis (ICA), and removed noisy components by manual inspection.

\paragraph{Epoching}
Epochs from -1500 to 1500 ms around stimulus onset were extracted to prepare the data for time–frequency analysis. A longer window gives more stable estimates at lower frequencies, reducing edge problems, and leaving enough pre-stimulus data for baseline correction and normalization. As theta is a low frequency oscillation, it requires a longer temporal window to be estimated reliably. Time-frequency estimates depend on having enough data around the event because edge artifacts can affect wavelet-based analysis \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}. I marked trials for rejection after epoching if the electrodes of interest (Fz, FCz, and Cz) exceeded physiological limits ($\pm 100\,\mu V$), as recommended by \textcite{luck2014erp}. Restricting the rejection criterion to the electrodes of interest prevented noisy channels outside the region of analysis from causing unnecessary trial loss. Rejection was done only after visual inspection.

\paragraph{Theta extraction}
Theta power was extracted from midfrontal electrodes (Fz, FCz, Cz) using time–frequency decomposition (newtimef in EEGLAB). Power in the theta band (4–8 Hz) was averaged within a 200–500 ms post-stimulus window. For each participant and condition (repeat–congruent, repeat–incongruent, switch–congruent, switch–incongruent), theta values were averaged across time, frequency, and electrodes to yield a single summary measure per condition.

\paragraph{Event-related spectral perturbations (ERSPs)} were computed for each participant and condition using EEGLAB’s wavelet-based time–frequency decomposition (newtimef). Analyses were conducted on pre-segmented condition-specific datasets (e.g., repeat–congruent), derived from the epoching and subsetting pipeline.

For each dataset, data from the channel of interest (FCz, Fz and Cz) were extracted, and time–frequency decomposition was performed across epochs using Morlet wavelets with a constant cycle parameter ([3 0.8]). The analysis window spanned $-$1500 to 1500 ms relative to stimulus onset, matching the epoch definition. Power estimates were baseline-corrected using a pre-stimulus interval from $-$500 to $-$100 ms.

Single-subject ERSPs were computed without visualization and stored as 2D time–frequency matrices (frequency × time). These matrices were then concatenated across participants into a 3D array, allowing aggregation at the group level.

Grand-average ERSPs were obtained by averaging across the participant dimension. The resulting group-level time–frequency representations reflect mean stimulus-locked changes in oscillatory power for the given condition.

\section{DALL-E prompt for image generation}
2x2 grid illustration in expressive manga style, clean line art, high contrast, subtle screentones. Same female protagonist with brown hair, same outfit, same kitchen, identical camera angle and framing in all four panels. Clear panel borders, equal size, minimal background clutter. Visual storytelling through posture, gaze, and motion lines. No text inside panels. Facial expressions subtle and controlled, showing cognitive states (focused, strained, calm, disengaged), not anger or distress.

Top-left (high stability, low flexibility): focused, neutral expression, steady downward gaze, precise repetitive chopping of carrots. Pot boiling over in background, ignored. Strong narrow attention lines to cutting board. Clean, controlled workspace.

Top-right (high flexibility, low stability): slightly strained, concentrated expression, wide attentive eyes, no clenched teeth. Fragmented action, chopping carrots and onions at once. Head turning, gaze split across targets. Phone buzzing with soft noise lines. Pot boiling, she reacts just in time. Cluttered workspace, overlapping motion lines.

Bottom-left (high flexibility, high stability): calm, composed expression. Steady chopping, phone present but ignored. When pot boils, smooth and deliberate shift: slight turn, lifting lid. Minimal, directional motion lines. Clean, efficient workspace.

Bottom-right (low flexibility, low stability): disengaged, mildly confused expression, soft unfocused gaze. Half-chopped vegetables, knife paused mid-air. Phone present but not attended. Pot boiling over while she hesitates. Weak, broken motion lines. Messy workspace.

Subtle visual encoding: sharp lines = attention, wavy lines = distraction, clean vs cluttered workspace = control. Faint signal arrows between pot and head indicate updating (aligned, excessive, blocked, inconsistent). Consistent lighting and composition.
