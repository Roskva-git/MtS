\begin{center}
    \Large\textbf{Abstract}
\end{center}

\noindent
\textbf{Author:} Røskva Bjørgfinsdóttir\\
\textbf{Title:} Sleeping patterns and flexible minds: Disentangling the effects of habitual and nightly sleep on cognitive control\\
\textbf{Supervisor:} Christina Thunberg \\
\textbf{Co-supervisor:} René Huster
\vspace{0.5cm}

Sleep is important for cognitive functioning, but it is still unclear whether naturalistic variation in sleep relates differently to different components of cognitive control. This thesis examined whether habitual sleep duration, sleep variability, and night-before sleep deviation were associated with behavioural and neural indices of cognitive control in a task-switching paradigm. The study used data from the Mind the Sleep project. Behavioural and EEG task effects were examined in 50 adults, while sleep-related mixed-effects models were restricted to participants with complete sleep data (\(N = 46\)). Sleep was assessed using daily sleep diaries. Cognitive flexibility was operationalized as switch costs, and cognitive stability as congruency effects. Frontal midline theta power was extracted from EEG as mean 4--8 Hz event-related spectral perturbation power from 200--500 ms after stimulus onset, averaged across Fz, FCz, and Cz.

The task produced clear behavioural effects. Participants were slower and less accurate on switch trials than repeat trials, and less accurate on incongruent than congruent trials. Reaction time and accuracy also showed switch \(\times\) congruency interactions, indicating that the task successfully manipulated both switching and interference demands. In contrast, theta power did not show reliable modulation by switch, congruency, or their interaction. Sleep-related mixed-effects models showed nominal interactions between sleep variables and switch condition for several outcomes. Longer-than-usual sleep before testing and greater sleep variability were associated with smaller reaction time switch costs. Habitual sleep duration and sleep variability were also nominally associated with switch-related accuracy and theta differences, but in opposite directions. No sleep-related fixed effects survived Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate correction, and no consistent sleep-related effects were observed for congruency.

Overall, the findings provide strong behavioural evidence for switch and congruency costs in the task, but only tentative evidence that naturalistic sleep variation relates to cognitive control. The sleep-related effects appeared more often for switch-related outcomes than for congruency-related outcomes, which was not the expected pattern. Frontal midline theta did not function as a clear marker of task demand in the present analysis. These results suggest that associations between everyday sleep and cognitive control may be more complex and weaker than expected, and should be interpreted cautiously. Larger samples, repeated EEG sessions, and more detailed sleep measurement are needed to clarify whether habitual and nightly sleep variation differentially affect cognitive flexibility and stability. \newline



\textbf{Keywords:} sleep, executive function, actigraphy, cognitive control, task switching

\chapter{Introduction}

Sleep is a fundamental biological process that supports cognitive and emotional functioning. Even in healthy adults, sleep duration and quality will differ across individuals and across time, shaped by circadian rhythms, lifestyle factors, and environmental demands \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Habitual sleep patterns may reflect relatively stable individual differences, whereas nightly deviations can reflect transient changes in arousal, fatigue, and regulatory capacity \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Understanding how these different aspects of sleep relate to cognition is important because everyday performance often depends on long-term sleep habits as well as sleep quality in the days leading up to a demanding situation.

There is an extensive body of research linking sleep and executive function, where two conceptual gaps are relevant for the present thesis. First, it is unclear how robust the effects of sleep on cognitive control are, and whether findings from controlled sleep deprivation studies extend to more naturalistic sleep. Much of the literature relies on acute manipulations of sleep loss, and it remains uncertain to what extent these findings generalize to functioning in daily life. In addition, cognitive control is not a unitary construct, and different components, such as stability and flexibility, may be affected in different ways. It is therefore unclear whether sleep has similar or distinct effects across these aspects of control. This also raises the question of whether different aspects of sleep, such as continuity and variability, modulate these components in the same way. Even when studies use objective and ecologically valid measures of sleep, fewer distinguish between habitual sleep level, that is, how a person typically sleeps over days or weeks, and sleep variability, that is, how much sleep fluctuates from night to night. These dimensions may have different cognitive correlates. Habitual sleep may reflect more stable characteristics, whereas variability may capture instability in sleep timing and quality that is relevant for cognitive control \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. This distinction is therefore important for understanding whether stable sleep patterns and night-to-night fluctuations relate differently to cognitive control.



\section{Stability and flexibility}

We rely on executive functions in everyday life, and especially when navigating novel or demanding situations, such as planning tasks or solving problems creatively \parencite{diamond2013executive, frith2021creativity}. When our executive functions fail, we can be easily distracted, for example by our phones in meetings, or experience odd glitches like putting a used plate in the fridge instead of in the dishwasher. Lapses in attention, difficulties updating behaviour, or an inability to suppress irrelevant information can also have more serious consequences, like impairing decision-making in contexts like academic performance, clinical judgement or traffic safety. Understanding the mechanisms that support flexible, goal-directed behaviour is a central challenge in cognitive neuroscience. Executive functions are often described as higher-order processes that enable us to regulate thoughts and actions in accordance with internal goals. A highly influential account is the unity and diversity framework proposed by \textcite{friedman2017unity}, which reconciles two seemingly competing views: that executive functions reflect a common control capacity, and that they consist of partially separable components. According to this model, tasks requiring inhibition, shifting, and updating share variance because they draw on a domain-general ability to actively maintain goals and bias processing toward task-relevant information. At the same time, each component captures additional processes that are not fully reducible to this shared control factor. Shifting tasks, for example, involve mechanisms related specifically to disengaging from one task set and activating another. This perspective implies that cognitive control can not be completely explained as a single unified faculty, nor is it a collection of entirely independent skills. Instead, executive functions can be seen as coordinated processes that operate together to support adaptive behaviour. 


Shifting can be understood as the process of disengaging from a previously relevant task set and activating a new one. This corresponds to cognitive flexibility, defined as the ability to update task representations in response to changing demands. In contrast, cognitive stability refers to maintaining and shielding the currently relevant task set from interference. Traditionally, these have been treated as the opposite end of a single dimension, implying that increased flexibility comes at the cost of reduced stability. However, contemporary frameworks contend that flexibility and stability are not necessarily opposite ends of a single scale, but could instead reflect coordinated processes that regulate when to maintain a goal and when to update it \parencite{egner2023principles, nack2023metacognition}. In everyday life, cognitive flexibility supports switching between rules, adapting to new information, and shifting attention between competing task sets \parencite{diamond2013executive, friedman2017unity}. When flexibility fails, behaviour becomes rigid, error-prone, or inefficient, especially in situations that require rapid reconfiguration of task goals and response mappings. Stability refers to maintaining task-relevant representations and shielding them from distraction (like chopping vegetables for dinner), whereas flexibility refers to updating those representations when task demands change (stopping the pot from boiling over). Mechanistically, the coordination of set shifting has been described in terms of biasing and gating processes in working memory. Biasing prioritizes currently relevant representations, while gating determines when new information is allowed to update the current task set. Drawing on pharmacological neuroimaging studies, \cite{cools2019gatingmechanisms} proposes that prefrontal dopamine supports the stable maintenance of task representations, whereas striatal dopamine enables flexible updating through different gating mechanisms. Engaging these control processes seems to be effortful and associated with subjective cost, and individuals tend to avoid recruiting control unless it is required or rewarded \parencite{kool2010cognitivecost, cools2016cognitivecost}. This applies to control states more generally, suggesting that variability in control also can reflect changes in motivation and resource availability rather than just the underlying capacity. These neural correlates do not necessarily lend more support to a unidimensional or dual dimension framework of set shifting.

\cite{nack2023metacognition} make a case for a dual-dimensional view of set shifting within a framework of metacontrol, defined as context-dependent regulation of cognitive control. Within this framework, flexibility and stability are conceptualized as metacontrol states instead of fixed capacities. They argue that manipulations of flexibility within participants do not consistently produce corresponding changes in stability. For example, increasing the frequency of task switches reliably reduces switch costs, indicating increased flexibility, but does not necessarily increase congruency effects, which would be expected if stability were reduced \parencite{geddert2022tradeoff}. In some cases, individuals adapt to increased demands for switching without showing any change in interference from irrelevant information. Similar patterns are observed when flexibility is manipulated through training or affect \parencite{zhao2020switchtraining}. Improvements in switching performance do not appear to come at the cost of reduced stability. Increasing flexibility does not make people more susceptible to interference from irrelevant information \parencite{sali2021flexibleattention}. Together, these findings suggest that flexibility can be upregulated without a corresponding decrease in stability, which is difficult to reconcile with a strict trade-off account.
Flexibility and stability do not show the negative relationship predicted by a unidimensional framework in between-participant studies either. If the two processes were strictly opposed, individuals with higher flexibility should consistently show lower stability. Instead, individual differences often affect one component without systematically influencing the other. For instance, factors such as training, bilingual experience, or emotion regulation can improve switching performance without altering interference effects \parencite{christoeffels2015bilingualflexible, mcrae2012reappraisalcontrol}. Conversely, some factors affect stability without changing flexibility. When asked, participants will often say they are either good or bad at both shifting attention and keeping focus, and they rarely see these skills as needing to be traded off against each other \parencite{derryberry2002anxiouscontrol}. Factor analyses have also shown that stability and flexibility load onto two factors and not one, meaning that they seem to reflect partly independent processes \parencite{bejjani2022latentswitch, chiorri2017factoranalysis}.

Evidence for this dissociation is also found at the neural level. Neuropsychological studies show double dissociations, where damage to different brain regions selectively impairs either flexibility or stability. For example, lesions to the superior medial frontal cortex are associated with increased perseverative errors, indicating impaired flexibility, whereas lesions to the inferior medial frontal cortex are associated with increased set-loss errors, indicating impaired stability \parencite{stuss2000doubledissociation}. A study by \cite{denouden2013dissociablelearning} showed dissociable effects of dopaminergic and serotonergic systems on reversal learning. Variation in serotonin transporter function selectively influenced lose-shift behaviour, reflecting sensitivity to negative feedback. Variation in dopamine transporter function influenced perseveration after reversal, reflecting the tendency to rely on previously learned associations. Since these effects did not overlap, updating based on immediate feedback and the persistence of prior learning could be supported by partly separable mechanisms. Both within-individual manipulations and between-individual differences, as well as dissociations in neural correlates, seem to suggest that flexibility and stability are not necessarily locked in a trade-off. Instead, they can vary independently depending on task demands, context, and individual characteristics. This distinction is important for the present thesis, as it implies that different aspects of cognitive control may be differentially sensitive to variations in sleep, rather than being affected uniformly.


\section{Measuring stability and flexibility}

When operationalizing cognitive stability and flexibility in task-switching paradigms, we try to bridge the gap between underlying constructs and their behavioural indicators. In everyday life, executive functions like shielding focus or shifting between tasks are complex, integrated, and context-dependent. These processes involve multiple interacting components, including goal maintenance, attentional selection, working memory updating, response selection and inhibition, meaning that multiple executive processes are engaged. In order to study executive functions in the lab, we need to reduce the complexity as much as possible by isolating specific components of behaviour. \textcite{rogers1995taskswitch} and \textcite{kiesel2010review} both highlight that defining a general rule of what constitutes a task set is difficult. Experiments will typically use something like categorizing letters or numbers, reading words, naming colours or objects. Participants get a specified rule to follow, if stimulus X then action Y. Often the stimulus is bivalent, meaning that it can elicit two different responses depending on the active set rule. Cognitive flexibility is operationalized as the ability to update or reconfigure task sets, indexed by switch costs, whereas cognitive stability is operationalized as the ability to maintain a task set in the presence of interference, indexed by congruency effects. These measures provide tractable and quantifiable proxies for otherwise multifaceted processes. However, this reduction comes at a cost. Because task performance reflects the combined contribution of several cognitive processes, these measures are not process-pure. For example, switch costs may reflect task-set reconfiguration, but it can also reflect interference from previous trials or differences in task preparation. Congruency effects can reflect both attentional control and stimulus–response competition \parencite{egner2023principles}. This issue, often referred to as task impurity, complicates the interpretation of behavioural indices as direct measures of stability and flexibility. In addition, the simplified and repetitive nature of laboratory tasks limits their ecological validity. Real-world cognitive control involves dynamically changing goals, richer contextual information, and self-initiated behaviour, which are only partially captured by controlled experimental paradigms. As such, task-switching measures should be understood as constrained operationalizations of broader executive functions rather than direct reflections of everyday cognitive control.

Task-switching paradigms are often used to investigate cognitive flexibility because they capture the ability to disengage from one task set and configure another in response to changing demands \parencite{monsell2003switching,kiesel2010review, rogers1995taskswitch, egner2023principles}. In the task switching paradigms like the one used in this thesis, participants alternate between simple classification rules. We then quantify flexibility using behavioural measures such as  accuracy and reaction times. Typically, participants will produce slower and less accurate responses on rule switching trials than on repetition trials. The switch cost is the difference in response time and/or accuracy between switch trials and repeat trials. Slower and more error-prone responses on switch trials are interpreted as reflecting the cognitive demands associated with updating task sets, including reconfiguration processes and interference from the previously relevant rule. Smaller switch costs are taken to indicate greater cognitive flexibility, or increased “switch readiness” \parencite{egner2023principles}.
Some task-switching designs also manipulate stimulus congruency, to investigate stability. Congruent stimuli afford the same response across task rules, whereas incongruent stimuli activate competing response mappings. That is, if a task is congruent it will elicit the same response, like a left button press, for both task rules. If the stimuli is incongruent it will elicit different responses for the different task rules, like a left button click for one rule and a right button click for the other. Congruency effects provide an additional measure of interference control to the switch cost in the set shifting task. There are some claims that interference is often amplified on switch trials, suggesting that the need to update task sets may temporarily increase susceptibility to competing stimulus–response associations \parencite{kiesel2010review}. If flexibility and stability load onto two different factors instead of one, we would expect that they are not inherently constrained by each other.

Task-switching paradigms can also combine switching and conflict manipulations. In these combined paradigms, congruent stimuli afford the same response across task rules, whereas incongruent stimuli activate competing response mappings. This allows for the simultaneous assessment of flexibility (via switch costs) and stability (via congruency effects) within the same task. Across these paradigms, behavioral performance is typically indexed using response times (RTs) and accuracy rates, from which switch costs and congruency effects are derived. Both switch costs and congruency effects are relative measures that reflect differences between conditions rather than absolute performance. Together, these measures provide complementary indices of the efficiency with which individuals update task sets and protect them from interference.


\section{Electrophysiological correlates}

Electroencephalogram (EEG) is commonly used to investigate cognitive conflict and set shifting \parencite{luck2014erp, cohen2013thetaconflict, cavanagh2015metatheta, cavanagh2014theta, depestele2023thetadriving}. When we measure brain activity we work from certain assumptions about brain anatomy and physiological mechanisms. EEG is a non-invasive and cost-effective measure of electrical activity in the brain. The brain is composed of grey and white matter, which are broadly organized in similar ways across individuals, although the brain is highly plastic and can adapt to structural variation. Grey matter contains densely packed neuronal cell bodies, whereas white matter primarily consists of myelinated axons that facilitate efficient communication between regions by increasing conduction speed. Neural communication involves both electrical and chemical processes. Within a neuron, electrical signals are propagated as action potentials, which arise from changes in membrane potential due to the movement of ions across the cell membrane. At rest, neurons have a membrane potential of approximately $-70$ mV, maintained by differences in ion concentrations, with higher concentrations of potassium inside the cell and sodium outside. When an action potential is generated, voltage-gated ion channels open, allowing sodium ions to enter the cell, leading to rapid depolarization, followed by repolarization as potassium exits the cell \parencite[Chapter 2]{gazzaniga2025principles}. EEG does not measure individual action potentials, but reflects the summed postsynaptic potentials of large populations of active neurons in the cortex. These signals propagate through brain tissue, cerebrospinal fluid, skull, and scalp before being detected by electrodes on the scalp. As a result, EEG has high temporal resolution but relatively low spatial resolution. This allows us to track the timing of neural processes with millisecond precision, even though the exact spatial sources of the signals are less precisely localized \parencite[Chapter 3]{gazzaniga2025principles}. 


At the neural level, cognitive flexibility is supported by a distributed frontoparietal control network involving medial and lateral prefrontal regions as well as posterior parietal cortex \parencite{kiesel2010review}. These regions are implicated in goal maintenance, attentional allocation, and the resolution of interference, all of which are required when behaviour must be reconfigured in response to changing task demands. Successful set shifting depends on the ability to represent multiple task rules, as well as mechanisms that monitor conflict and signal the need for increased control \parencite{botvinick2001conflict}. Conflict processing has traditionally been studied using event-related potentials (ERPs), which reflect phase-locked neural responses to stimulus onset. One well-established component is the N2, a negative deflection peaking approximately 200–300 ms after stimulus onset. The N2 component is thought to index conflict detection processes occurring between stimulus evaluation and response execution. Conflict related neural activity can also be captured by increases in midfrontal theta-band power (FM-theta, 4–8 Hz), which largely reflect non-phase-locked oscillatory dynamics. Event-related oscillatory activity can be distinguished into evoked and induced components. Evoked activity is phase-locked to stimulus onset and therefore preserved in the averaged ERP, whereas induced activity varies in phase across trials and largely cancels out during averaging \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}. As a result, ERP analyses primarily reflect phase-locked responses and may underestimate oscillatory dynamics that are not strictly time-locked. Time–frequency analysis is an alternative that is applied at the single-trial level prior to averaging, allowing both evoked and induced activity to be captured in measures of total activity, often referred to as event-related spectral perturbation (ERSP).
Time–frequency analyses have shown that theta-band power can persist after removal of the ERP, suggesting partially distinct neural processes \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}, with theta activity being more closely linked to behavioral performance \parencite{cohen2013thetaconflict}.

Frontal midline theta (FM-theta) is a non-phase-locked rhythmic power change typically observed over medial frontal scalp sites. Converging evidence from EEG and intracranial recordings suggests that FM-theta is primarily generated in the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and adjacent medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), regions implicated in performance monitoring and the dynamic allocation of control \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,depestele2023thetadriving}. FM-theta is thought to index a domain-general control signal that increases when ongoing behaviour requires adjustment. Accordingly, FM-theta power is enhanced under conditions that place elevated demands on control, including response conflict, error commission, and task switching. Switch trials and incongruent trials often elicit stronger theta activity than low-conflict conditions, suggesting that medial frontal systems register the need for increased control when competing task sets or response tendencies are activated \parencite{cohen2013thetaconflict, cavanagh2014theta}. In this sense, FM-theta can be understood as a neural signature of control engagement during flexible behaviour.


\section{Stable ability or context-sensitive?}

Executive functions (EF) have been conceptualized both as stable cognitive capacities and as context-sensitive control processes that vary across situations. From a trait-perspective, EF is conceptualized as a relatively stable capacity that supports goal-directed behaviour across domains. This view is supported by the unity–diversity framework proposed by \textcite{friedman2017unity}. Using latent variable approaches, they show that the executive functions inhibition, updating, and shifting are both separable and correlated, reflecting both a common underlying factor as well as domain-specific components. In their latent variable analysis, the letter–number switching task loads moderately on the shifting factor ($\lambda \approx$.66), indicating that performance on this task partly reflects an underlying trait-like shifting ability. However, a considerable proportion of the variance remains task-specific ($\approx$ .56), meaning that performance is also influenced by processes that are unique to the task itself. Each individual task in the factor analysis, like the letter–number switching task, reflects a mixture of three things. Part of the performance reflects an underlying ability we are interested in, such as shifting. Another part reflects factors that are specific to that task, such as perceptual demands, strategies and momentary attention. The third part reflects residual noise. In this way, no single task is a pure measure of an executive function. When we use a latent variable approach, we look at how performance on several different tasks relates to each other. If a person performs well on one shifting task, they also tend to perform well on other shifting tasks. The part of performance that is consistent across these tasks is treated as shared variance. This shared variance is interpreted as reflecting the underlying executive function, because it is the component that generalizes across different task formats. We can aggregate the tasks by statistically extracting what multiple tasks have in common, rather than by simply averaging scores. By isolating this shared component across many tasks, we can reduce the influence of task-specific noise. What remains is a latent factor, such as the Common EF  \textcite{friedman2017unity} propose, which is more stable over time and across contexts than performance on any single one task. When the common variance across tasks is isolated, a stable underlying control capacity becomes visible. Individual tasks on their own are much noisier and more influenced by context. Even paradigmatic measures of control, such as the stop-signal task, exhibit low test–retest reliability, with substantial variation across sessions that cannot be explained by measurement noise alone. This pattern suggests that commonly used behavioural indices of control are strongly influenced by situational factors and may not reflect stable traits to the extent often assumed \parencite{thunberg2024unreliability}. The unity-diversity framework implies that variability in task performance does not necessarily reflect changes in the underlying executive function itself.  Fluctuations in performance can instead arise from state-dependent factors that influence how control is deployed in a given context. These influences are not captured by the latent factor itself, but remain in the task-specific and residual variance.

Further support for the trait view comes from behavioural genetics. Executive functions are found to be highly heritable at the latent level and influenced by polygenic factors, indicating that stable biological differences contribute to variation in control abilities \parencite{friedman2011genetic}. Longitudinal studies show that early differences in self-control predict later outcomes across domains such as academic achievement, health, and social functioning. The classic example is the marshmallow test, where delay of gratification in early childhood has been linked to life outcomes decades later \parencite{mischel1988marshmallow}. These findings have been widely interpreted as evidence that executive control reflects a stable, domain-general capacity. Even though executive functions show properties such as heritability, stability over time and predictive validity, the picture may be more nuanced. For example, in strategic decision-making, participants need to determine how much evidence to accumulate before committing to a response, like when deciding whether dots on a screen are moving to the left or right. \textcite{bogacz2010speedaccuracy} describe this process as the gradual accumulation of noisy evidence toward a decision threshold. The process starts from a given starting point, which can be biased toward one of the response options, and continues until the accumulated evidence reaches a threshold that triggers a response. A higher threshold requires more evidence, leading to slower but more accurate decisions, whereas a lower threshold allows for faster but more error-prone responses. Individuals tend to show relatively consistent response tendencies, reflected in stable starting points or general levels of caution. At the same time, these parameters can be adjusted depending on task demands, allowing individuals to trade speed for accuracy when needed. The starting point can be understood as reflecting a stable response bias, whereas changes in threshold reflect flexible control. This suggests that behaviour is shaped both by relatively stable biases and by flexible, context-dependent adjustments that are implemented online in response to current goals.

As for the predictive validity of EF traits, reinterpretations of classic findings challenge the notion that early behaviour reflects intrinsic, stable self-control capacity. Reanalyses of the marshmallow test indicate that its predictive validity may be better explained by social and environmental factors, such as trust and socioeconomic context, rather than stable individual differences in willpower. In this view, children's willingness to delay gratification reflects adaptive responses to their environment rather than fixed control abilities \parencite{michaelson2020marshmallow}. Contemporary models of cognitive control emphasize its adaptive and dynamic nature. Rather than reflecting a fixed capacity, control is thought to be continuously adjusted based on task demands, recent experience, and expected outcomes. Neurocognitive accounts described in \textcite{cavanagh2015metatheta}'s metareview suggest that frontal midline theta signals index the need for control and coordinate its implementation across brain networks, supporting a view of control as an online, context-sensitive process rather than a static trait. Metacognitive accounts further reinforce the state-based perspective by emphasizing that individuals monitor their own performance and allocate control accordingly. Control is costly and selectively deployed, increasing in situations of conflict, uncertainty, or error, and decreasing when demands are low. This selective allocation implies that variability in performance may reflect differences in control engagement rather than differences in underlying capacity \parencite{cavanagh2015metatheta}. Similarly, frameworks such as the dual mechanisms of control and adaptive control theories describe control as flexibly shifting between modes depending on situational demands \parencite{egner2023principles, nack2023metacognition, cavanagh2014theta}.

Taken together, these findings suggest that while stable individual differences in executive function clearly exist, they do not fully account for observed behaviour. Executive control appears to emerge from an interaction between relatively stable predispositions and dynamically changing contextual influences. In this sense, cognitive control can be studied as a flexible system that adapts to current demands, internal states, and environmental constraints.


\section{Sleep as a potential contextual moderator}

Sleep is a fundamental biological process that supports cognitive and emotional functioning. Even in healthy adults, sleep varies both between individuals and across time, reflecting the joint influence of circadian rhythms, homeostatic regulation, and environmental demands \parencite{foster2020sleep}. Sleep emerges from coordinated activity across large-scale neural networks. It is divided into stages that are characterized by different patterns of neural oscillatory activity, which is believed to support processes including synaptic plasticity and memory processing \parencite{adamantidis2019oscillating}. Insufficient or disrupted sleep has been related to a host of negative outcomes for both mental and physical health \parencite{foster2020sleep}.
A large body of research has linked sleep loss with impaired cognitive performance in domains like attention and executive functioning \parencite{lim2010meta, lowe2017meta, sen2023sleepef, tai2022sleepef, slama2018sleepswitching, wilckens2020sleepmddswitch, wilckens2014sleeppreparation, fjell2024sleep, kusztor2019differentialsleep, wilckens2016sleepcognitionage, basner2013sleepcognition, couyoumdjian2010switchingsleep}. Experimental studies suggest that sleep deprivation disproportionately affects sustained attention, goal maintenance, and performance monitoring, while more basic processes such as response inhibition and conflict detection appear relatively preserved \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep, killgore2010sleepcognition}. This pattern indicates that sleep loss selectively disrupts processes that depend on continuous allocation of cognitive resources and the active maintenance of task-relevant information. This distinction is directly relevant for the components of cognitive control described earlier. Cognitive stability refers to the ability to maintain task-relevant representations and shield them from interference, a function that depends on sustained attention and goal maintenance. Because these processes are consistently shown to be vulnerable to sleep loss, existing evidence suggests that sleep may primarily affect the stability component of cognitive control. In contrast, cognitive flexibility involves updating task sets in response to changing demands. While such updating can be effortful and control-dependent, it may also be partially driven by stimulus–response associations or contextual cues, making its relationship with sleep less straightforward. Sleep deprivation has also been associated with diminished theta responses during cognitively demanding tasks, alongside behavioural impairments in executive functioning \parencite{wilckens2014sleeppreparation,wilckens2020sleepmddswitch}. At the same time, growing evidence challenges the assumption that a single optimal sleep duration applies to everyone. Individual sleep needs appear to be flexible and dynamically related to cognitive functioning, suggesting that both trait-like differences and state-dependent fluctuations should be considered when evaluating sleep–cognition associations \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. Night-to-night variability is common, also among healthy adults, and may carry cognitive consequences that are not captured by average sleep estimates. Discrepancies between subjective and objective sleep measures is another factor that points to the complexity of accurately characterizing sleep behaviour \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Given that sleep loss appears to selectively affect processes such as sustained attention and goal maintenance, one possibility is that sleep primarily influences cognitive stability, leading to increased congruency effects. In contrast, effects on flexibility, as indexed by switch costs, may be weaker or less consistent. However, because flexibility may depend on both controlled and more automatic processes, it is also possible that sleep affects both components, or that effects vary depending on task demands. Empirical evidence directly comparing these components in relation to naturalistic sleep is currently limited.

Sleep is studied in a number of ways. Sleep duration is one of the most studied dimensions, and refers to the total time spent asleep. Cohort studies suggest a quadratic relationship between sleep duration and executive functioning, with both short and long sleep associated with poorer performance, and approximately seven hours linked to more optimal outcomes \parencite{tai2022sleepef, sen2023sleepef}. However, duration alone provides an incomplete picture. Sleep efficiency, typically defined as the proportion of time in bed spent asleep, reflects how consolidated sleep is. Continuity captures the degree to which sleep is fragmented by awakenings, while variability refers to night-to-night fluctuations in sleep patterns. Emerging evidence suggests that these qualitative aspects may be at least as important as total sleep time when considering cognitive consequences \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Sleep quality also appears to have broad implications for cognitive processes central to executive functioning. Experimental sleep deprivation often impairs sustained attention, slows reaction time, and reduces working memory capacity. Individuals who are sleep deprived also tend to rely more on habitual responses rather than goal-directed control, suggesting reduced cognitive flexibility \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. These effects are not limited to extreme deprivation. Poor sleep efficiency and fragmented sleep have been linked to worse executive function and increased risk of later cognitive decline, indicating that everyday variations in sleep can meaningfully influence cognitive performance \parencite{sen2023sleepef}. Most evidence linking sleep and cognitive control is derived from acute sleep deprivation paradigms. While these studies provide strong causal evidence, they rely on extreme manipulations that may not generalize to everyday functioning. In naturalistic settings, sleep varies continuously, both in terms of habitual levels and night-to-night fluctuations. Emerging work suggests that both average sleep characteristics and variability in sleep may be relevant for cognitive performance, although their specific contributions remain unclear \parencite{fjell2024sleep, sen2023sleepef}.
Despite extensive evidence linking sleep to executive functioning, it remains unclear whether naturalistic variations in sleep differentially affect cognitive stability and flexibility, and how these effects are reflected in neural markers of control. Few studies have combined objective, longitudinal sleep measures with both behavioral and electrophysiological indices within the same task. This limits our understanding of how stable sleep patterns and night-to-night fluctuations relate to these components of cognitive control in everyday contexts.



\section{The current study}

This study examines whether variation in sleep is associated with variation in cognitive control. Prior work has largely relied on acute sleep deprivation paradigms, making it unclear whether similar patterns are observed under naturalistic conditions. In addition, cognitive control is not a unitary construct, and it remains unresolved whether sleep relates similarly to different components such as stability and flexibility, or whether these are differentially affected. The present study addresses these gaps by linking longitudinal sleep measures to behavioural and neural indices of cognitive control within the same task. Sleep is characterized along two dimensions: habitual sleep level, reflecting average sleep over time, and sleep variability, reflecting night-to-night fluctuations. Cognitive control is assessed using a task-switching paradigm that allows simultaneous measurement of flexibility (switch costs) and stability (congruency effects), alongside frontal midline theta as a neural marker of control engagement.

The study asks whether stable sleep patterns and short-term fluctuations are associated with behavioural performance and neural activity during cognitive control, and whether these associations differ between stability and flexibility. It is expected that poorer sleep, reflected in lower habitual sleep time or greater variability, will be associated with reduced cognitive control. \newline

\subsection{Study design}
The study was a within-subjects design with 3 sessions separated by 2 weeks. At the first session, participants were screened for depression using a MINI clinical interview. They filled out baseline questionnaires, and were instructed in the use of a daily sleep diary. Participants reported their sleep continuously over four weeks. The two subsequent sessions involved EEG recordings during resting state and cognitive tasks assessing executive functions (stop-signal, task-switching, Stroop, and n-back).

\paragraph{Sample}
The project included adult participants (18-65 years), both with and without symptoms of depression. All participants were right-handed, spoke Norwegian, and had normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Participants were screened for depressive symptoms using the MADRS during a telephone interview before the first session. The initial sample consisted of N = 50 participants. For sleep-related analyses, two participants were excluded because of missing sleep diaries, and two were excluded due to missing sleep data on the night prior to testing, leaving a sample of N = 46.

Participants were excluded if they had a history of neurological or severe psychiatric disorders (e.g., psychotic disorders, bipolar disorder), significant head injury, or use of certain medications. Individuals with high depressive symptom scores (MADRS $>$ 20) were only included if they were receiving treatment for their depression.

\subsection{Data acquisition}

We recorded EEG using a multichannel amplifier (BrainAmp, Brain Products GmbH, Germany) with 31 passive Ag/AgCl electrodes positioned according to the international 10–20 system. FCz served as the online reference and AFz as ground, with earlobe electrodes used for offline re-referencing. Electrode impedances were kept below 5 $k\Omega$. Data were recorded using BrainVision Recorder (Brain Products GmbH, Germany) at a sampling rate of 5000 Hz. Stimuli were presented on an Eizo FlexScan S2411W monitor (1920 \(\times\) 1200 resolution, 60 Hz refresh rate) at a viewing distance of approximately 70 cm. The experiment was run on a Dell Precision T5500 computer (Dell Inc., USA) using MATLAB (The MathWorks Inc., USA) with Psychophysics Toolbox 3. Participants used a Cedrus RB-740 response pad (Cedrus Corporation, San Pedro, USA) to respond to the tasks.

Each EEG session contained a resting state recording (5 minutes with eyes open, 5 minutes with eyes closed) and four tasks in the following order: stop signal task (for 20 minutes), task-switching task (15 minutes), Stroop task (12 minutes), and the n-back task (25 minutes). The order was kept constant across participants and sessions. The first EEG-session recording of the task switching task was used in the present thesis. The executive function each task is meant to assess is: response inhibition (stop-signal task; \parencite{logan1984inhibition}), cognitive flexibility (task-switching paradigm; \parencite{rogers1995taskswitch}), interference control (color–word Stroop task; \parencite{stroop1935interference}), and working memory updating (n-back; \parencite{kirchner1958update}).

\paragraph{Task-Switching Paradigm}
In the task-switching task (see figure \ref{fig:taskswitchingparadigm}, table \ref{tab:task_switching} for response options), participants were presented with a pair of one letter and one number on the screen for 1200 ms. The font colour of the pair indicated which rule to follow, and was either blue or yellow. If the font colour was blue, then participants were instructed to report if the number was even (left button press) or odd (right button press). If the font colour was yellow, participants were instructed to report if the letter was a vowel (left button press) or a consonant (right button press). Prior to the presentation of the pair, a fixation cross was presented on the screen for a duration between 1300 and 1700 ms.

The trial types were either switch or repeat and either congruent or incongruent. In one-third of the trials, the font colour switched from the colour in the previous trial: the switch trials. In the remaining two-thirds of the trials, the font colour was the same as the previous trial: the repeat trials. Congruent trials were the ones where the different tasks would elicit the same correct response. For example, letter-number combinations of AEIYU and 02468 (vowels and even numbers) would be congruent whether the font colour was yellow or blue, because they would both elicit a left button press as the correct answer. Incongruent trials were the ones where participants would have to press different buttons for correct responses if the font colour changed, such as letter-number pairs consisting of e.g. AEIYU and 13579 (vowels and odd numbers), where the correct response for yellow font would be a left button press and correct response for blue font would be a right button press.
The task consisted of four blocks, with each block consisting of 60 trials. Participants had 15 trials for practicing before the experiment began. In total, the task consisted of 240 trials, adding up to a total duration of approximately 15 minutes. After each block, participants got feedback on their response. If their accuracy was less than 60\%, the message "Try to be more accurate" was displayed. With mean reaction times longer than 850ms they got the message "Try to respond faster". If accuracy exceeded 60\% and mean reaction time was below 850 ms, they were shown the message "Well done".


\subsubsection{EEG data processing}

For the preprocessing pipeline, I applied a low-pass filter at 40 Hz, resampled the data to 500 Hz, then applied a high-pass filter at 0.1 Hz and removed EMG channels. I then mapped electrode names and added FCz as the empty reference electrode, before re-referencing to the average of the earlobes. After that I ran an independent component analysis (ICA), and removed noisy components by manual inspection. Then I created an event list based on the logfiles, and renamed the triggers for epoching. 
Epochs from -1500 to 1500 ms around stimulus onset were extracted to prepare the data for time–frequency analysis. A longer window gives more stable estimates at lower frequencies, reduces edge problems, and leaves enough pre-stimulus data for baseline correction and normalization. As theta is a low frequency oscillation, it requires a longer temporal window to be estimated reliably. Time-frequency estimates depend on having enough data around the event because edge artifacts can affect wavelet-based analysis \parencite{herrmann2014timefrequencytutorial}. I marked trials for rejection after epoching if electrodes exceeded physiological limits of ($\pm 200\,\mu V$), as recommended by \textcite{luck2014erp}. Midfrontal electrodes Fz, FCz, and Cz were chosen as the region of analysis (ROI) because frontal midline theta in cognitive-control paradigms is typically maximal over the medial frontal scalp. I then inspected the ROI channels visually. Restricting the rejection criterion to the electrodes of interest prevented noisy channels outside the ROI from causing unnecessary trial loss. Rejection was done only after the visual inspection.

\paragraph{Theta extraction and ERSP computation}
Time–frequency decomposition was performed in EEGLAB using newtimef on the full 3-second stimulus-locked epochs. Analyses were conducted on condition-specific datasets corresponding to the four experimental conditions: repeat–congruent, repeat–incongruent, switch–congruent, and switch–incongruent. For each participant and condition, ERSPs were computed separately for the midfrontal electrodes Fz, FCz, and Cz using Morlet wavelets with parameter [3 0.8], a prestimulus baseline of $-500$ to $-100$ ms, an explicit initial window length of 1 s, and a frequency range of 3–25 Hz. ERSPs were then averaged across the three electrodes to get one time–frequency representation per participant and condition. Grand-average ERSPs were obtained by averaging these matrices across participants. Theta power was operationalized as mean ERSP power in the 4–8 Hz band from 200 to 500 ms after stimulus onset, averaged across time, frequency, and the three midfrontal electrodes, resulting in one summary value per participant and condition.

For descriptive comparison with the time-frequency results, stimulus-locked ERPs were also extracted from the cleaned epoched datasets. After rejection of flagged artifact epochs, baseline correction was applied using the interval from $-200$ to 0 ms. ERPs were then computed separately for the four experimental conditions (repeat--congruent, repeat--incongruent, switch--congruent, and switch--incongruent) by averaging across trials and across the midfrontal electrodes Fz, FCz, and Cz. Grand-average ERPs were obtained by averaging the resulting condition-specific waveforms across participants.


\subsection{Sleep diaries}
Sleep was assessed using daily entries in the Bergen Sleep Diary, completed continuously across four weeks between the first and subsequent laboratory sessions. The diaries were used as the primary source of sleep data in all analyses. Participants reported bedtime, time they attempted to fall asleep, sleep onset latency, nocturnal awakenings, and final morning awakening. From the diary entries, nightly total sleep time (TST), sleep onset latency (SOL), wake after sleep onset (WASO), sleep efficiency (SE), and number of awakenings were derived. TST was calculated as the interval between attempted sleep time and final morning awakening, minus reported sleep onset latency and nocturnal wake time, measured in hours. When participants did not report any awakenings, WASO was coded as zero. WASO comprises both the time it took participants to fall asleep as well as how long they were awake during nightly awakenings, measured in minutes. Sleep onset latency is how long it took participants to fall asleep, measured in minutes. Sleep efficiency is the percentage of time from the participants went to bed until their final awakening, in other words it is the proportion of wake after sleep onset out of the total sleep time.

The primary sleep variable used in the present analyses was diary-based total sleep time (TST). Habitual sleep was operationalized as each participant's mean TST across the recording period. Nightly sleep deviation was calculated by centering each night's TST relative to the participant's own mean TST, so positive values reflected longer-than-usual sleep and negative values reflected shorter-than-usual sleep. For analyses of behavioural and EEG outcomes, the relevant within-person predictor was the TST deviation from the night immediately preceding each EEG session. These measures were used to capture both between-person differences in habitual sleep and within-person variation in sleep prior to testing.
Actigraphy was collected in parallel with the sleep diaries, but was not used as a primary analytic measure in the present thesis. I used the actigraphy reports to verify diary entries and identify implausible sleep periods. Any apparent sleep periods not supported by the actigraphs were excluded from further consideration. The sleep analyses reported here reflect diary-based sleep estimates and not actigraphy-derived sleep parameters.



\section{Statistical Analysis}

I conducted statistical analyses to examine task-related effects in behavioural performance and frontal midline theta, as well as associations between sleep and behavioural and neural outcomes. Behavioural and EEG task-effect analyses were based on the full available sample, whereas sleep-related analyses were restricted to participants with complete sleep data. Behavioural and EEG data were first examined using repeated-measures analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with the within-subject factors Switch (switch vs. repeat) and Congruency (congruent vs. incongruent). A 2 \(\times\) 2 repeated-measures ANOVA tests whether mean differences across four within-subject conditions, defined by two factors with two levels each, are larger than expected by chance while accounting for within-participant variability. Separate ANOVAs were conducted for reaction time, accuracy, and theta power. I assessed behavioural performance using reaction times and accuracy. For each participant, reaction times were averaged separately within the four task conditions, repeat--congruent, repeat--incongruent, switch--congruent, and switch--incongruent. Only correct-response trials were included in reaction time analyses in order to measure the speed of successful task performance. Both incorrect trials and omission trials, defined as failures to respond within the 1200-ms response window, were excluded from reaction time analyses. Accuracy analyses treated both incorrect responses and omissions as unsuccessful trials. EEG analyses were also restricted to correct trials, so neural estimates reflect processing during successful task performance and not error- or omission-related activity. For each participant and condition, theta was operationalised as mean event-related spectral perturbation (ERSP) power in the 4--8 Hz band from 200 to 500 ms after stimulus onset, averaged across the midfrontal electrodes Fz, FCz, and Cz. This resulted in one theta value per participant for each of the four task conditions.

Sleep was operationalised along three dimensions derived from the sleep diaries. Habitual sleep duration is the mean total sleep time across the recording period (Mean TST). Sleep variability is the within-participant standard deviation of total sleep time (TST SD) from their own average. Night-before sleep deviation is the deviation in total sleep time from the participant's own mean on the night preceding EEG assessment (TST dev).

Associations between sleep and task performance were examined using mixed-effects models with random intercepts for each participant. Mixed-effects models, sometimes called multi-level or hierarchical models, allow us to model both fixed effects (experimental conditions, sleep predictors) that apply to the whole sample, and random effects (participant-level intercepts and slopes) that capture individual baseline differences. Since the data in the current thesis contain repeated observations per participant, mixed models can account for within-subject correlation and use all available observations without aggregating to condition means. Separate models were fitted for reaction time, accuracy, and theta. For reaction time and theta, I used linear mixed-effects models. For accuracy, I used a generalized linear mixed-effects model with binomial distribution and logit link. Correct trials were coded as successes and incorrect responses plus omissions were coded as failures. In all models, switch, congruency, and one sleep predictor were entered together with their interactions. Separate models were fitted for Mean TST, TST SD, and TST dev. For the models using habitual sleep duration and sleep variability, predictors were grand-mean centred prior to analysis. Night-before deviation scores were already centred relative to each participant's own mean by definition. The repeated-measures ANOVAs were based on the available behavioural and EEG samples (N = 50), and sleep-related mixed-effects models were restricted to participants with complete data for sleep predictors and task outcomes (N = 46). According to a two-tailed sensitivity analysis in G*Power ($\alpha$ err prob: .05, power: 0.80) there were enough participants left after exclusion to detect moderate correlations (r = 0.40) in the sleep-related analyses, but not small effects. Statistical significance was evaluated at $\alpha = .05$. Mixed-effects models were specified a priori to test whether sleep variables were associated with performance and with task-related effects of switching and congruency. Because several sleep-related fixed effects were tested across models, Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate correction was applied to the sleep-related effects in the nine sleep models. Both uncorrected \(p\)-values and FDR-adjusted \(q\)-values are considered in the interpretation.

The plots show baseline-corrected changes in spectral power relative to the pre-stimulus period, with the 4--8 Hz theta band marked by horizontal lines. Across conditions, there was visible post-stimulus theta-band activity, but the overall pattern was similar across the four conditions. This visual pattern fits the ANOVA results, which did not show reliable modulation of theta power by switch, congruency, or their interaction. As a descriptive sanity check, stimulus-locked ERPs were also inspected across conditions. The ERP waveforms are shown in Appendix~\ref{fig:erp}. \section{Sleep-related mixed-effects models}

For each outcome (RT, accuracy and theta) I fitted mixed-effects models with switch, congruency, one sleep predictor, and their interactions. Each mixed model tested whether sleep predicted overall performance (main effect) and whether sleep modulated switching and/or congruency effects (interactions). Models included a random intercept for participant to account for between-subject baseline differences and the repeated-measures structure (four condition observations per participant). Full model formulas and outputs are provided in the Appendix. Because the accuracy models used a logistic link, estimates are reported on the log-odds scale and should be interpreted as directional effects on the probability of a correct response. Since all the mixed models are sleep-related, I used N = 46 participants, to limit the sample to participants with full usable sleep data and to make the models comparable. Sleep-related effects appeared primarily as interactions with switching (i.e., modulation of switch-related effects) and not with congruency or theta. Night-before deviations (TST dev) predicted reaction time switch cost, with longer sleep being related to smaller cost, but it not predict accuracy or theta. Habitual mean total sleep time and sleep variability produced opposite-direction interactions on accuracy and theta, with longer mean total sleep time being associated with larger accuracy switch-costs, but a more positive switch-related theta difference. Greater total sleep time variability was associated with smaller accuracy switch-costs and a reduced switch-related theta difference. Model estimates reported below are given in seconds but converted to ms where helpful for interpretation. Effect sizes and confidence intervals are reported in the Appendix mixed-model tables.


\subsection{Reaction time}

Three mixed-effects models were fitted for reaction time, with TST deviation, mean TST, and TST SD entered in separate models. In the model with deviation from total sleep time the night before EEG, the switch \(\times\) TST deviation interaction was significant (\(b = -0.022\), \(SE = 0.010\), \(t = -2.123\), \(p = .035\)). Because reaction time was modelled in seconds, this estimate corresponds to about 22 ms less switch-related slowing for each additional hour of sleep the night before relative to the participant's average. There was no main effect of TST deviation and no congruency-related sleep interactions. Mean total sleep time did not significantly predict reaction times overall, nor did it interact with switch or congruency. For the sleep variability model, the switch \(\times\) TST SD interaction was significant (\(b = -0.046\), \(SE = 0.022\), \(t = -2.08\), \(p = .039\)). Greater habitual variability in TST was associated with reduced switch-related slowing (\(\approx 46~\mathrm{ms}\) less switch cost per additional hour of TST SD). No congruency-related interactions reached significance. Night-to-night increases in sleep before testing were associated with smaller switch costs, and greater long-term variability was likewise associated with smaller switch reaction time costs. Habitual mean sleep duration did not predict reaction time.

\subsection{Accuracy}

Three logistic mixed-effects models were fitted for accuracy, with TST dev, mean TST, and TST SD entered in separate models. In the model investigating sleep deviation the night before the EEG-session, there were no significant interactions between sleep deviation and switch or congruency, indicating that night-before sleep deviation did not reliably predict accuracy across task conditions. Accuracy effects were dominated by the main task effects of switch and congruency. 
In the model investigating the effect of habitual sleep, mean TST, there was a significant switch \(\times\) mean TST interaction, \(b = -0.190\), \(SE = 0.078\), \(p = .016\). The main effect of mean TST was not significant. On the log-odds scale this indicates that higher habitual sleep duration was associated with a larger switch-related reduction in accuracy (i.e., greater accuracy cost on switch trials relative to repeat trials), while mean TST did not predict overall accuracy. 
In the model examining sleep variability, there was a significant switch \(\times\) TST SD interaction, \(b = 0.402\), \(SE = 0.171\), \(p = .020\). The main effect of TST SD was not significant, and no congruency-related sleep interactions were significant. Greater sleep variability was associated with a smaller switch-related reduction in accuracy (i.e., less drop in accuracy on switch trials). No congruency-related sleep interactions were significant. Habitual mean sleep and variability were related to switch-specific accuracy changes, but in opposite directions. Longer habitual sleep predicted larger switch accuracy costs, whereas greater sleep variability predicted smaller switch accuracy costs.

\subsection{Theta}

Three mixed-effects models were fitted for theta power, with TST deviation, Mean TST, and TST SD. In the model analyzing sleep deviation the night before EEG, TST dev, there were no significant interactions between sleep deviation and switch or congruency, and the main effect of TST dev was not significant. Night-before sleep deviation did not reliably predict theta power. 
In the model investigating habitual sleep, Mean TST, there was a significant switch \(\times\) Mean TST interaction, \(b = 0.417\), \(SE = 0.189\), \(t = 2.203\), \(p = .029\). The main effect of Mean TST was not significant. Participants with higher habitual total sleep time showed a more positive switch–repeat theta difference (i.e., greater switch-related theta increase).
In the model including sleep variability, TST SD, there was a significant switch \(\times\) TST SD interaction, \(b = -0.918\), \(SE = 0.420\), \(t = -2.186\), \(p = .030\). Again, the main effect of TST SD was not significant, and no congruency-related sleep interactions were significant. Higher sleep variability was associated with a less positive switch–repeat theta difference.
No congruency-related sleep interactions were significant for theta. Habitual sleep and sleep variability modulated theta specifically in relation to switching (switch vs. repeat), in directions that mirror the accuracy results. Longer habitual sleep was linked to larger switch-related theta increases, while greater variability was linked to smaller switch-related theta increases. Night-before deviations did not reliably predict theta.


\chapter{Discussion}
The purpose of the present study was to investigate the relationship between sleep and executive functions, and more specifically whether habitual or nightly sleep influences cognitive flexibility. The thesis also investigates whether these sleep mechanisms differentiate between a unidimensional or a dual dimensions view of set shifting, by trying to tease cognitive flexibility and stability apart. To shed light on these questions, we collected daily sleep diary data from our participants, as well as inviting them to EEG sessions. Cognitive flexibility and stability operationalized through a task-switching paradigm that separated two within the same task. Stability was operationalized as congruency effects, meaning poorer performance on incongruent than congruent trials. Flexibility was operationalized as switch costs, meaning poorer performance on switch than repeat trials. Habitual sleep was measured in total sleep time, and daily sleep was measured in sleep deviation the night before testing and sleep variability from individual mean sleep time. Sleep was used as predictors and were entered into mixed-effects models for reaction time, accuracy, and theta activity.

Based on previous studies showing that sleep deprivation disproportionately affects sustained attention and goal maintenance, I hypothesized that poorer sleep would be associated with larger congruency effects, reflecting reduced cognitive stability. The task itself produced clear congruency effects, with incongruent trials being slower and less accurate than congruent trials. However, none of the models reached statistical significance for any of the congruency-related sleep interactions. In other words, the study found evidence that the congruency manipulation worked behaviourally, but not that poorer sleep amplified congruency costs.
The second hypothesis was that association between sleep and switch costs should be weaker or less consistent than the sleep-congruency effects, because previous studies have shown that basic processes such as response inhibition and conflict detection appear relatively preserved after sleep deprivation. This would reflect a smaller effect of sleep on cognitive flexibility. This was partly contradicted by the results. The task produced very clear switch costs in both reaction time and accuracy, and the sleep-related effects appeared more often for switch-related outcomes than for congruency-related outcomes. Longer-than-usual sleep before EEG showed nominal associations with reduced switch-related slowing in reaction time, and greater sleep variability was also nominally associated with reduced switch-related slowing. In accuracy, both habitual sleep duration and sleep variability interacted with switch condition. Rather than sleep effects being mainly tied to stability, the nominal results in this thesis pointed to sleep being consistently related to switch-related processes. Frontal midline theta was expected to increase with task demands, with higher theta in incongruent and switch trials, and poorer sleep was expected to be associated with lower theta overall. This hypothesis was not supported. Descriptively, theta was highest in the switch-congruent condition, but the ANOVA showed no significant main effect of switch, no significant main effect of congruency, and no switch × congruency interaction. Unlike the behavioural outcomes, theta did not show reliable task-related modulation. Mean total sleep time and sleep variability did not show significant main effects on theta, but both showed switch-related interactions before FDR correction. No congruency-related theta interactions were significant. As such, theta did not behave as the expected general marker of reduced control recruitment under poorer sleep, and the only sleep-theta associations were tied to switching.

The present thesis found behavioural evidence that the task captured both flexibility and stability demands, but the sleep hypotheses were not supported in the expected direction. Sleep did not mainly affect congruency effects, and theta did not reliably increase under higher task demands. The most coherent pattern was that the nominal sleep-related effects were concentrated around switch-related performance and switch-related theta variation, while congruency-related sleep effects were largely absent. In this sample, sleep was not a clear predictor of cognitive stability or flexibility, and frontal midline theta did not provide evidence for the proposed neural mechanism.



\section{Stability and flexibility}

The clearest behavioural finding was that the task produced robust switch costs and congruency effects. Participants were slower and less accurate on switch trials than repeat trials, and less accurate on incongruent trials than congruent trials. This suggests that the task successfully captured both components targeted in the thesis: flexibility, operationalised as switch costs, and stability, operationalised as congruency effects. The sleep-related results, however, did not follow the expected pattern. Based on previous sleep deprivation research, I expected poorer sleep to be more strongly associated with congruency effects, reflecting reduced stability or poorer shielding of the active task set. Instead, the nominal sleep-related effects were concentrated around switch-related outcomes, whereas congruency-related sleep interactions were largely absent. This pattern should be interpreted cautiously, because none of the sleep-related effects survived FDR correction. Still, the distribution of the nominal effects is informative for the theoretical question. Switch costs are commonly interpreted as reflecting the demands of changing from one task set to another, including task-set reconfiguration, interference from the previously relevant rule, and preparation for the currently relevant response mapping \parencite{rogers1995taskswitch,monsell2003switching,kiesel2010review}. In the present study, sleep variables showed nominal interactions with switching for reaction time, accuracy, and theta, but not with congruency. This may suggest that, in this sample and task context, naturalistic variation in sleep was more closely related to processes involved in updating or reconfiguring task sets than to processes involved in shielding the current task set from interference.

The absence of congruency-related sleep effects is also important. Congruency effects were clearly present behaviourally, indicating that incongruent trials were more demanding than congruent trials, but the effects did not seem to be reliably modulated by sleep. This does not necessarily mean that cognitive stability is unaffected by sleep. It could suggest that the degree of naturalistic sleep variation observed here was not sufficient to produce detectable changes in congruency-related performance. One possibility is that congruency effects in this task rely partly on relatively robust stimulus--response competition processes, whereas switching depends more strongly on transient control processes involved in updating task rules. If so, switch costs may be more sensitive to fluctuations in arousal, fatigue, or control engagement than the congruency effect measured here. This interpretation aligns partly with the literature on sleep deprivation. Previous studies suggest that sleep loss affects sustained attention, goal maintenance, and effortful top-down control, while some more automatic control processes appear relatively preserved \parencite{kusztor2019differentialsleep}. I originally expected this to produce stronger associations between poor sleep and congruency effects, because stability depends on maintaining task goals and resisting interference. The present findings did not support that prediction. Instead, the nominal effects suggest that sleep may have been more relevant for the flexible updating of task sets than for interference control. This could mean that naturalistic sleep variation does not map neatly onto findings from acute sleep deprivation studies. It is also possible that flexibility and stability are affected differently depending on whether sleep is manipulated experimentally or measured as an everyday, naturally varying behaviour.
The results in the present study do not provide strong evidence that sleep selectively affects either flexibility or stability, but they do suggest a tentative pattern in which sleep-related variation was more visible for switch-related outcomes than for congruency-related outcomes. This could reflect the context-sensitive aspect of cognitive control, where performance varies both with stable executive ability and with current state factors such as sleep, arousal, and task engagement. Since the sleep-related effects were not robust enough to survive correction for multiple testing, it could mean that we would need a larger sample of participants to see if the trends reflect real sleep effects on cognitive control.

A possible mechanistic way to interpret the concentration of nominal sleep-related effects around switching is through state-dependent variation in task-set updating or gating. Task switching requires participants to disengage from the previously relevant task set and configure the currently relevant rule. In the present task, this meant updating whether the stimulus should be classified according to the number or the letter. Switch costs could then reflect task-set reconfiguration, interference from the previous rule, and the updating of stimulus--response mappings \parencite{rogers1995taskswitch,monsell2003switching,kiesel2010review}. Within frontostriatal gating accounts, prefrontal regions are thought to support the active maintenance of task goals, whereas striatal gating mechanisms regulate when new information is allowed to update the current task set \parencite{cools2019gatingmechanisms}. From this perspective, switch trials may place stronger demands on transient updating and gating than repeat trials. If naturalistic sleep variation affects arousal, fatigue, or the efficiency with which control is recruited, these effects may be more visible in switch-related outcomes than in congruency effects. In contrast, congruency effects may partly reflect stimulus--response competition within the currently active task set, which may have been less sensitive to the range of sleep variation observed in this study. Since we did not measure frontostriatal activity directly, and none of the sleep-related fixed effects survived FDR correction, the findings in this thesis cannot be taken as evidence for sleep modulated frontostriatal gating. But the pattern provides a possible theoretical link between the observed concentration of nominal switch-related effects and models that distinguish between maintaining a task set and updating it when demands change.




\section{Habitual sleep and variability}

The sleep-related findings also raise the question of why habitual sleep duration and sleep variability appeared to show different patterns. Habitual sleep (mean TST) and sleep variation (TST SD) were included to capture different aspects of sleep. Habitual sleep was reflected in how much a participant typically slept across the recording period, and sleep variability captured how much sleep duration varied from night to night. The values of these variables cannot be universally interpreted as interchangeable indicators of ``better'' or ``worse'' sleep. Habitual sleep duration is meant to reflect relatively stable individual differences in sleep need, lifestyle, chronotype, or baseline arousal, while sleep variability reflects instability in sleep routines, irregular schedules, or flexibility in sleep behaviour. This distinction is consistent with the idea that individual sleep needs are not fixed around one universal optimum, but vary across individuals and contexts \parencite{fjell2024sleep}. In the present study, the nominal effects of habitual sleep duration and habitual sleep variability pointed in different directions for switch-related accuracy and theta. Higher mean total sleep time was associated with a larger switch-related reduction in accuracy, but also with a larger switch-related theta difference. Higher sleep variability, on the other hand, was associated with a smaller switch-related reduction in accuracy and a smaller switch-related theta difference. Because these effects did not survive FDR correction, they should not be interpreted as strong evidence for opposite effects of habitual sleep and sleep variability. Still, the pattern suggests that average sleep duration and sleep variability may capture partly different participant characteristics. A possible interpretation of these effects is that habitual sleep reflects relatively stable individual differences in sleep need or cognitive strategy. Participants with longer total sleep time on average may not necessarily be ``better rested'' than participants with shorter habitual sleep. They may simply need more sleep to reach a comparable level of functioning. If so, longer mean total sleep time could mark a subgroup with different baseline arousal, fatigue vulnerability, or task strategy. The combination of larger switch-related theta and larger switch-related accuracy costs could be interpreted as greater control recruitment under switching demands, but not necessarily more efficient performance. In this view, higher theta may reflect increased effort or compensatory engagement rather than better control.

A related possibility is that the sleep-related pattern reflects differences in speed--accuracy strategy. Decision-making models describe performance as depending partly on how much evidence is accumulated before a response is made. A more cautious response strategy increases the amount of evidence required before responding, whereas a less cautious strategy allows faster but potentially more error-prone responding \parencite{bogacz2010speedaccuracy}. Although the present study did not model latent decision parameters directly, this framework provides a possible interpretation of the behavioural findings. Participants with longer habitual sleep may have adopted a strategy involving greater control engagement during switch trials, reflected in higher switch-related theta, while still showing larger accuracy costs under switching demands. Conversely, participants with more variable sleep may have shown smaller measured switch costs because of a different response strategy or lower phasic control engagement, rather than because sleep variability was beneficial. This interpretation is speculative since the analyses that were carried out in this thesis cannot distinguish between sleep need, sleep quality, chronotype, lifestyle regularity, motivation, or response strategy. The sleep measures were based on diaries, and diary-based total sleep time could partly reflect subjective reporting habits in addition to actual sleep duration. Unmeasured variables such as time of day, caffeine use, work schedules, mood, or depressive symptoms could also contribute to the observed associations. In addition, executive-function task measures are known to be influenced by both trait-like and state-like factors, and common behavioural indices of control may show limited temporal stability \parencite{thunberg2024unreliability}. The apparent differences between mean total sleep time and sleep variability should be interpreted as a possible pattern to follow up, and not as evidence that habitual sleep duration and sleep variability have opposite causal effects on cognitive flexibility. The present analyses cannot determine whether these patterns reflect cognitive control differences, response strategies, or broader state factors such as arousal and task engagement. Further analytic approaches could be pursued to separate stable individual differences from occasion-specific variation, and behavioural model approaches could separate accuracy, speed, and response caution more directly.



\section{Midfrontal theta}
Frontal midline theta was included as a neural marker of control engagement, based on previous work linking theta activity to conflict monitoring, task demands, and the need for cognitive control \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,cohen2013thetaconflict}. In the present study theta did not show the expected task-related pattern. The repeated-measures ANOVA showed no significant main effect of switch, no significant main effect of congruency, and no switch \(\times\) congruency interaction. This means that theta did not function as a clear general marker of task demand in the present analysis, even though the behavioural task effects were robust. There are several possible reasons for this null result. Theta power varied substantially between participants, especially in the switch conditions, which may have reduced sensitivity to condition-level differences. The number of usable trials also varied by condition, with incongruent switch trials being the lowest. This could have made theta estimates less stable. The theta measure used in the present thesis was also a relatively broad summary measure, defined as mean 4--8 Hz power from 200--500 ms across Fz, FCz, and Cz. This window was theoretically motivated, but individual differences in the timing, frequency, or scalp distribution of theta responses could have blurred condition effects when averaged across participants. The absence of significant theta task effects does not necessarily mean that theta was irrelevant to task performance, but it does mean that the present analysis did not provide strong evidence for the expected task-related theta modulation.

The sleep-related theta models showed a different, but still tentative, pattern. Habitual sleep and sleep variability showed nominal switch \(\times\) sleep interactions, but sleep did not interact with congruency. Longer habitual sleep durations tended to be associated with a larger theta increase during switch trials. When participants’ sleep duration varied more from night to night, theta increased less on switch trials compared with repeat trials. These nominal effects mirror the broader pattern in which sleep-related associations were concentrated around switch-related outcomes and not with congruency-related outcomes. A possible interpretation is that the switch-related theta findings reflect differences in control engagement. Frontal midline theta has been proposed to signal the need for control and to support coordination of control across task-relevant networks \parencite{cavanagh2014theta,cavanagh2015metatheta}. From this perspective, the larger switch-related theta difference when participants had longer habitual sleep could reflect greater recruitment of control during switch trials. Increased theta can reflect efficient control engagement, but it can also reflect greater effort or greater experienced demand, so more theta activity does not automatically translate to better performance. At the group level, the nominal associations showed that higher habitual sleep duration was linked to larger switch-related theta differences, but not to better switch-related accuracy. This could mean that theta reflected effort, task demand, or compensatory engagement and not more efficient cognitive control. Higher sleep variability was associated with a smaller switch-related theta difference, while also being associated with smaller switch-related accuracy costs. This could reflect reduced phasic control engagement, a different response strategy, or lower sensitivity of the theta measure in participants with more variable sleep. It does not necessarily mean that sleep variability improves cognitive control. Rather, it suggests that sleep variability and habitual sleep duration may relate differently to neural and behavioural indices of switching, and that theta alone is not sufficient to determine whether these patterns reflect better control, greater effort, or strategic differences in task performance.

Overall, the theta findings were weaker than expected. Behaviourally, the task clearly elicited switch and congruency effects, but theta did not show reliable task-related modulation. The nominal sleep-related theta interactions suggest a possible link between habitual sleep patterns and switch-related control engagement, but this interpretation remains tentative. Future work could test this more directly by modelling both EEG sessions while accounting for session and practice effects, and by exploring whether participant-specific theta windows provide more sensitive estimates of frontal midline theta.

\section{Unitary or dual-dimensional cognitive control?}

One of the broader aims of this thesis was to examine whether sleep-related variation would support a unitary or a more differentiated view of cognitive control. A strictly unitary account would predict that a contextual factor such as sleep would affect cognitive control more globally, producing similar associations with switch costs, congruency effects, and theta activity. In a dual-dimensional account, flexibility and stability can vary partly independently. From this perspective, sleep could be associated with one component of control without necessarily affecting the other. The present results cannot rule out one account or the other, but it might fit more closely with a dual dimensions framework, although only tentatively. 

Behaviourally, the task produced both switch costs and congruency effects, showing that both flexibility and stability demands were present. At the same time, the nominal sleep-related effects were concentrated around switch-related outcomes, while congruency-related sleep interactions were largely absent. This suggests that naturalistic sleep variation may have been more closely related to processes involved in task-set updating or reconfiguration than to interference control. In this sense, the findings are more consistent with a view of cognitive control as composed of partly separable processes than with a fully unitary control construct. At the same time, there are several arguments against the support for a dual dimensions framework that can be drawn from the results. None of the sleep-related effects survived FDR correction. The study also used behavioural proxies for flexibility and stability rather than direct measures of the underlying mechanisms. When operationalizing cognitive mechanisms we try to tease apart behaviour to reach appropriate levels of explanation, but making a task process-pure is difficult. Switch costs can reflect preparation, task-set reconfiguration, and interference from the previous task, while congruency effects can reflect several forms of stimulus--response competition. The findings in this thesis show that sleep-related variation did not affect all control measures uniformly, but they do not establish that flexibility and stability are fully independent systems.

What the results do support is the broader idea that cognitive control is context-sensitive. Task performance appeared to depend both on the experimental manipulation itself, and on individual differences in sleep patterns, and possibly on state-dependent factors such as arousal, effort, and strategy. This fits with accounts of executive function in which stable control abilities coexist with task-specific and occasion-specific variation \parencite{friedman2017unity}. In this sense, the present thesis does not reject a unity-and-diversity view of executive function. Rather, it adds to the argument that single-task performance reflects both common control demands and more specific processes that may be differentially sensitive to contextual factors such as sleep.




\section{Limitations and future research}

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the present findings. First, the sleep-related analyses were based on a modest sample size (\(N = 46\)). The sensitivity analysis indicated that this sample was sufficient to detect moderate bivariate associations, but not small effects. The main sleep-related hypotheses were tested as interaction effects in mixed-effects models, which generally require more power than main effects. As mentioned before, none of the sleep-related fixed effects survived FDR correction, and the nominal switch-related sleep effects should be interpreted as tentative patterns rather than robust evidence that sleep modulated cognitive control. The absence of congruency-related sleep effects should also not be interpreted as strong evidence that sleep is unrelated to stability, but rather as evidence that no reliable association was detected in the present sample.

A second limitation concerns the use of only one EEG session in the present thesis. The broader Mind the Sleep project included two EEG sessions, but the current analysis focused on the first task-switching EEG session. This was a deliberate scope decision, because including both sessions would introduce additional sources of variance, including session order, practice effects, time between sessions, and possible changes in EEG data quality. Using only one EEG session limited the ability to test within-person sleep effects. TST deviation was defined relative to each participant's own average sleep duration, but because task performance and theta were analysed from a single session, the model could not test whether a participant performed differently on nights after sleeping more or less than usual. Future analyses could extend the present work by modelling both EEG sessions while explicitly accounting for session and practice effects. This would allow a stronger test of whether night-before sleep deviations are associated with corresponding within-person changes in reaction time, accuracy, or theta. The limitation of using only one EEG session is also relevant for the broader distinction between stable individual differences and state-dependent variation. A central idea in the thesis is that cognitive control may reflect both relatively stable abilities and context-sensitive fluctuations. With the current design I could not fully separate these sources of variance. Latent state--trait modelling would be one possible framework for this question, because it is designed to separate stable trait variance from occasion-specific state variance and measurement error \parencite{steyer1999lst}. With only two EEG sessions, however, a model like this would be limited because it could estimate stable between-person differences and occasion-specific residual variance, but it would not be able to cleanly separate trait, state, and error components in the way that would be possible with three or more measurement occasions. Future studies with at least three EEG sessions would therefore be better suited for testing whether sleep-related variation in cognitive control reflects stable individual differences, temporary states, or measurement noise.

The sleep measures also have limitations. Sleep was assessed using daily sleep diaries, with actigraphy used to verify diary entries and identify implausible sleep periods. This approach allowed sleep to be studied across several weeks in a relatively naturalistic setting, but diary-based TST is still partly subjective. Participants may differ in how accurately they estimate sleep onset, awakenings, and final awakening, and diary-based sleep may not correspond perfectly to actigraphy or polysomnography. This is important because mean TST and TST SD do reflect sleep duration and variability, but they could also reflect reporting habits, lifestyle, chronotype, mood, or daily schedule. Future work should test whether the present pattern holds when using actigraphy-derived sleep variables, and whether diary and actigraphy measures explain overlapping or different aspects of cognitive performance. A more detailed sleep design could also examine whether specific sleep features, such as sleep efficiency, sleep fragmentation, slow-wave sleep, or REM sleep, are more closely related to cognitive control than total sleep time alone.

Another limitation concerns the measurement of cognitive control. The task-switching paradigm allowed flexibility and stability to be examined within the same task, using switch costs and congruency effects as behavioural indices. As already mentioned, these measures are not process-pure. Switch costs may reflect task-set reconfiguration, preparation, interference from the previous task, response selection, and general slowing, while congruency effects may reflect several forms of stimulus--response competition \parencite{rogers1995taskswitch,kiesel2010review}. This means that the present results cannot identify the exact mechanism behind the nominal sleep-related switch effects. A smaller switch cost could reflect more efficient updating, but it could also reflect differences in response caution, motivation, or speed--accuracy strategy. Future work could address these questions by combining task-switching data with computational modelling. Drift-diffusion modelling could help separate response caution, information accumulation, and non-decision processes \parencite{bogacz2010speedaccuracy}. This would make it possible to test whether sleep variables are related to cognitive control itself, or whether the observed associations are better explained by differences in response strategy, arousal, or task engagement. Such modelling could be useful for interpreting the opposite nominal patterns observed for mean TST and TST SD in accuracy and theta.

Finally, the generalizability of the findings is limited by the sample and design. The sample consisted of adults with and without depressive symptoms, and the sex distribution was uneven. Potentially relevant factors such as depressive symptoms, age, sex, time of day, caffeine use, work schedule, chronotype, and trial counts were not included as covariates in the present models. These factors could influence both sleep and cognitive performance, and may partly account for the observed associations. Future studies could use larger samples and model relevant covariates more explicitly. Experimental sleep manipulation, such as within-subject sleep restriction or sleep extension, would also be needed to test causal effects of sleep on switching, congruency effects, and theta. The present findings should therefore be seen as a naturalistic first step: they suggest possible links between sleep patterns and switch-related cognitive control, but stronger designs are needed to determine whether these associations are reliable, causal, and mechanistically specific.



\chapter{Conclusion}

This thesis set out to examine whether everyday sleep variation helps explain differences in cognitive control, and whether these associations differ between flexibility and stability. The behavioural task effects were clear, with participants showing robust costs of both switching and incongruency. This means that the task successfully captured the control demands it was meant to capture. The sleep-related findings, however, were more tentative. Sleep did not show the expected pattern of stronger associations with congruency effects, and frontal midline theta did not show reliable task-related modulation. Counter to the expected findings, the nominal sleep-related effects were concentrated around switching rather than congruency. The thesis could not lend support to a unitary or a dual dimensions view of cognitive control. 

The thesis supports the value of studying sleep and cognitive control in a naturalistic framework, but also shows the difficulty of doing so. Everyday sleep is variable, cognitive-control measures are not process-pure, and neural markers such as frontal midline theta may not map cleanly onto behaviour in modest samples. Even so, the null findings contribute to the understanding that the everyday relationship between sleep and cognitive control is complex, and may differ from what we find in more controlled experiments. The central question of the thesis remains open. Future work is needed to test whether naturalistic sleep variation is relevant for task-set updating and flexible control, and whether this can be separated from response strategy, arousal, and individual sleep need.

Same female protagonist with brown hair, same outfit, same kitchen, identical camera angle and framing in all four panels. Clear panel borders, equal size, minimal background clutter. Visual storytelling through posture, gaze, and motion lines. No text inside panels. Facial expressions subtle and controlled, showing cognitive states (focused, strained, calm, disengaged), not anger or distress.

Top-left (high stability, low flexibility): focused, neutral expression, steady downward gaze, precise repetitive chopping of carrots. Pot boiling over in background, ignored. Strong narrow attention lines to cutting board. Clean, controlled workspace.

Top-right (high flexibility, low stability): slightly strained, concentrated expression, wide attentive eyes, no clenched teeth. Fragmented action, chopping carrots and onions at once. Head turning, gaze split across targets. Phone buzzing with soft noise lines. Pot boiling, she reacts just in time. Cluttered workspace, overlapping motion lines.

Bottom-left (high flexibility, high stability): calm, composed expression. Steady chopping, phone present but ignored. When pot boils, smooth and deliberate shift: slight turn, lifting lid. Minimal, directional motion lines. Clean, efficient workspace.

Bottom-right (low flexibility, low stability): disengaged, mildly confused expression, soft unfocused gaze. Half-chopped vegetables, knife paused mid-air. Phone present but not attended. Pot boiling over while she hesitates. Weak, broken motion lines. Messy workspace.

Subtle visual encoding: sharp lines = attention, wavy lines = distraction, clean vs cluttered workspace = control. Faint signal arrows between pot and head indicate updating (aligned, excessive, blocked, inconsistent). Consistent lighting and composition.
